## What is a mantis shrimp?

A mantis shrimp is a [marine crustacean](/topics/marine-animals) in the order Stomatopoda. Despite the name, it is not related to true shrimp or praying mantises—it belongs to a separate, ancient group that has existed for more than 400 million years.

The peacock mantis shrimp (*Odontodactylus scyllarus*) is the most well-known species, famous for its vivid rainbow colors, extraordinary eyes, and devastating strike.

## Where do mantis shrimp live?

Mantis shrimp live in warm tropical and subtropical oceans worldwide, most commonly in the shallow [coral reefs](/habitats/coral-reef) of the Indo-Pacific. They dig burrows in sandy seafloor, rubble, or rock crevices and ambush prey that passes by.

They are found from East Africa and the Red Sea across to Hawaii and throughout Southeast Asia, from the surface down to about 300 feet (90 m) depth. Explore more [reef animals](/topics/reef-animals).

## What do mantis shrimp eat?

Mantis shrimp are [carnivores](/diets/carnivore) that eat fish, crabs, mollusks, worms, and other crustaceans. There are two hunting strategies in the group.

**Smashers** have dense, calcified clubs and use them to shatter the hard shells of snails, crabs, and clams. **Spearers** have barbed, spear-like appendages to impale fast-moving soft prey like fish. A single mantis shrimp uses one strategy or the other depending on its species.

## The extraordinary eyes

Mantis shrimp have the most complex eyes in the animal kingdom. While humans have **3 types of color receptors**, mantis shrimp have **16**. They can detect ultraviolet light, infrared light, and several forms of polarized light completely invisible to people.

Surprisingly, research suggests they may not "see" more colors than we do—rather, their brains process color much faster using a simpler comparison system, ideal for lightning-quick hunting decisions. Read more about [animal senses](/topics/animal-senses).

## The most powerful punch in nature

A mantis shrimp's dactyl club accelerates at up to **10,000 g** and reaches about **50 mph (80 km/h)**—one of the fastest movements recorded in any animal. The strike is so forceful that it creates **cavitation bubbles** in the water around the impact.

These bubbles collapse instantly, generating a shockwave and a brief flash of heat near 8,000°F (4,400°C). Even if the first blow misses, the shockwave can stun or kill prey. This makes mantis shrimp uniquely dangerous for their size.

## Life cycle and reproduction

Mantis shrimp are attentive parents. After mating, the female (or in some species both parents) guards the egg mass and aerates it by fanning with their appendages until hatching. Larvae drift in the open ocean as plankton before settling and growing into adults.

Many mantis shrimp are **monogamous**, pairing for life and sharing the same burrow for years—an unusually loyal social bond for a crustacean. Young grow through multiple molts before reaching adult size.

## Conservation

Most mantis shrimp species are listed as [Least Concern](/conservation-status/least-concern). However, **coral reef degradation** from bleaching, pollution, and destructive fishing methods destroys the shallow reef and rubble habitat these animals depend on.

Mantis shrimp are also harvested for food markets across Southeast Asia and used in the aquarium trade. Protecting reef ecosystems is the single most important step toward keeping mantis shrimp populations healthy around the world.
