## What is a whale shark?

A whale shark is the **largest fish on Earth**. Even though its name includes the word "whale," it is a shark, not a mammal.

Its scientific name is *Rhincodon typus*. Whale sharks have a wide mouth, pale spots on dark skin, and gills for breathing underwater.

## Where do whale sharks live?

Whale sharks live in **warm oceans** around the world. They are often seen near the surface in tropical and subtropical seas, but they can also dive deep offshore.

In some places, many whale sharks gather seasonally where plankton or fish eggs are especially abundant.

## What do whale sharks eat?

Whale sharks are [carnivores](/diets/carnivore), but like manta rays they eat mostly tiny prey. They feed on **plankton, fish eggs, krill, and small fish** by filtering water through their gills.

Their mouths are huge, but they are not built for chasing large prey.

## How do whale sharks behave?

Whale sharks usually move in a calm, steady way through the ocean. They are often **solitary**, though they may gather in rich feeding areas.

People often call them gentle giants because they are not known for attacking humans.

## Life cycle and pups

Whale sharks give birth to live young called **pups**. Scientists still know surprisingly little about where the youngest whale sharks spend their early years.

That mystery matters because protecting nursery areas could be important for the species' survival.

## Why are whale sharks so large?

Their giant size may help them travel long distances, store energy, and feed efficiently on huge amounts of tiny prey. Thick skin and a powerful tail also help them move through the open sea.

Each whale shark has its own spot pattern, which helps researchers identify individuals.

## Why are whale sharks at risk?

Whale sharks are listed as **Endangered**. They face threats from fishing, accidental capture in nets, ship strikes, and pollution.

Because they grow slowly and live a long time, whale shark populations do not bounce back quickly after losses.
