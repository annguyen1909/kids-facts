## What is a snow leopard?

A snow leopard is a large wild cat built for life in the **highest mountains** in the world. It has thick pale spotted fur, a long bushy tail nearly as long as its body, and enormous paws that spread its weight across deep snow. Snow leopards are famously hard to see—earning them the nickname **"ghost of the mountains."**

Their scientific name is *Panthera uncia*. They belong to the cat family [Felidae](/families/felidae).

## Where do snow leopards live?

Snow leopards live in the **mountain ranges of Central and South Asia**. Their range covers the Himalayas, the Tibetan Plateau, the Karakoram, the Altai mountains, and surrounding ranges. Countries including China, Mongolia, Nepal, India, Pakistan, and Kyrgyzstan hold wild populations.

They typically live at elevations between **9,800 and 17,000 feet (3,000 to 5,200 m)**—higher than almost any other large cat. Their range shifts with the seasons as prey animals move up and down the slopes.

## What do snow leopards eat?

Snow leopards are [carnivores](/diets/carnivore). Their main prey includes **blue sheep** (bharal), Himalayan tahr, ibex, marmots, and hares. A snow leopard can kill prey up to three times its own body weight and may feed on a large kill for days.

Unlike other big cats, snow leopards are **ambush hunters** in extreme terrain. They use rocky ridges and cliffs to close the distance on prey before leaping with extraordinary power. Their hind legs are among the most powerful of any cat relative to body size.

## How do snow leopards live?

Snow leopards are **solitary** and cover large home ranges. They communicate through scent markings on rocks and boulders. Their activity peaks at dawn and dusk, when mountain prey is most active.

One unique behavior is **tail-wrapping**: resting snow leopards often drape their thick tail around their nose and face like a scarf, trapping warm air in freezing mountain temperatures. Read more about [solitary animals](/topics/solitary-animals).

## Life cycle and cubs

A baby snow leopard is called a **cub**. A mother gives birth to two or three cubs in a rocky den, often lining it with her own shed fur for warmth. Cubs are born blind and helpless.

Cubs stay with their mother for about **18 months**, learning to stalk and leap across uneven mountain terrain. Males play no role in raising cubs. Young snow leopards must find and hold their own mountain territory before hunting large prey alone.

## Why are snow leopards at risk?

Snow leopards are listed as **Vulnerable**. Illegal poaching for fur and bones, loss of prey animals, and conflict with herders who lose livestock to snow leopard attacks are the biggest threats. Climate change is also shrinking the cold mountain habitats that snow leopards depend on.

Conservation programs in countries across Central Asia train local communities to protect snow leopards. Camera trap surveys and GPS collars help researchers track individual cats across their vast mountain ranges.

## Snow leopard vs leopard

Snow leopards and leopards look similar but are distinct species. Snow leopards are **smaller and paler**, with larger spots arranged in open rosettes. Their tail is far longer than a leopard's and their paws are much wider.

Snow leopards cannot roar. They make unique sounds including a soft **"chuff"** or prusten used between individuals that know each other, and loud wailing calls during the breeding season.
