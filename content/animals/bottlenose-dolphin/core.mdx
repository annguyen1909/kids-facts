## What is a bottlenose dolphin?

A bottlenose dolphin is an intelligent **marine mammal** that lives in the ocean. It breathes air, gives birth to live young, and feeds milk to its calf. Dolphins are not fish—they are warm-blooded like humans.

Their scientific name is *Tursiops truncatus*. Bottlenose dolphins are among the best-known dolphins in the world.

## Where do bottlenose dolphins live?

Bottlenose dolphins live in **oceans, coasts, and bays** around the world, including waters near the United States, Australia, and South Africa. Many stay in warm or temperate **coastal waters** where fish are easier to find.

They travel in groups called **pods** along shorelines, estuaries, and open sea routes. Some populations move seasonally as water temperature and food change.

## What do bottlenose dolphins eat?

Bottlenose dolphins are [carnivores](/diets/carnivore). They eat fish, squid, and other small sea animals. Dolphins usually **swallow food whole** rather than chewing it.

They may chase fish, work with pod members, and use **echolocation** to find prey in murky water. A dolphin sends out sound clicks and listens for echoes bouncing off nearby objects.

## How do dolphins live in pods?

Bottlenose dolphins are [social animals](/topics/social-animals) that stay connected through whistles, clicks, and body movements. A **pod** is a group of dolphins that travel, feed, and rest together.

Dolphins are strong swimmers with streamlined bodies. They breathe through a **blowhole** on top of the head and must come to the surface for air. They can sleep with one half of the brain at a time so they keep swimming safely.

## Life cycle and calves

A baby dolphin is called a **calf**. After about 12 months inside the mother, a calf is born tail-first in the water. The mother feeds milk to the calf and teaches it to swim and hunt.

Calves stay close to their mothers and learn pod calls that help the group recognize one another.

## Are bottlenose dolphins at risk?

Bottlenose dolphins are listed as **Least Concern**, which means wild populations are generally stable in many regions. They still face threats from pollution, fishing nets, boat traffic, and habitat change.

Protecting clean oceans and coastal waters helps dolphins and the fish they depend on stay healthy.
