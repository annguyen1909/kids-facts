## What is a lionfish?

A lionfish is a striped **reef fish** with long, feathery fins and venomous spines. The red lionfish is one of the best-known species.

Its scientific name is *Pterois volitans*. Lionfish belong to a group of spiny fishes that use both warning colors and venom for defense.

## Where do lionfish live?

Red lionfish are native to the **Indo-Pacific**, where they live on coral reefs and nearby rocky habitats. They also use places such as mangroves and sheltered coastal waters.

They are now also well known as an **invasive species** in the Caribbean, Gulf of Mexico, and western Atlantic.

## What do lionfish eat?

Lionfish are [carnivores](/diets/carnivore). They eat **small fish, shrimp, crabs, and other reef animals**.

They often hunt by spreading their wide fins, cornering prey, and then sucking it in with a fast strike.

## How do lionfish behave?

Lionfish usually move slowly and hover around reef cover. They often hunt more actively at dusk or night.

Their bold stripes and venomous spines help keep many predators away.

## Life cycle and young

Lionfish reproduce quickly. Females release **floating egg masses**, and the eggs hatch into tiny larvae that drift with ocean currents.

That fast breeding is one reason lionfish spread so easily in places where they are invasive.

## Are lionfish dangerous?

Lionfish are not aggressive toward people, but their spines are **venomous**. A sting can be very painful.

Divers and fishers need to handle them carefully, especially when removing invasive lionfish from reefs.

## Why are lionfish a problem in some seas?

In their native range, lionfish are a normal part of reef ecosystems. In the Atlantic, however, they eat many native reef animals and can reduce young fish populations.

That is why some conservation programs focus on catching invasive lionfish and protecting reef communities from their spread.
