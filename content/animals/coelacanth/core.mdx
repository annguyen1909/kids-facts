## What is a coelacanth?

The coelacanth (*Latimeria chalumnae*) represents one of the greatest zoological discoveries of the twentieth century. For decades, the scientific community agreed that this ancient lineage of lobe-finned fishes vanished during the late Cretaceous extinction event 65 million years ago. That absolute certainty was shattered in December 1938 when Marjorie Courtenay-Latimer discovered a living specimen among a fisherman's catch in East London, South Africa.

Recognized instantly by its deep cobalt-blue scales, white camouflage specks, and primitive morphology, the coelacanth is the ultimate "living fossil." It provides an invaluable evolutionary window into the ancient transition from aquatic fishes to terrestrial four-legged tetrapods.

## Where does it live?

West Indian Ocean coelacanths primarily inhabit the steep marine volcanic slopes and deep underwater cave systems surrounding the Comoros Islands, South Africa, Tanzania, Madagascar, and Mozambique. A second, distinct species (*Latimeria menadoensis*) inhabits deep waters near Sulawesi, Indonesia.

These specialized fish reside in the twilight and dark zones of the ocean, typically at depths between 500 and 2,300 feet (150 to 700 meters). During the daylight hours, coelacanths gather peacefully in small groups inside cool, dark submarine caves where cold upwelling currents provide plentiful oxygen.

## What does it eat?

When the sun sets, coelacanths depart their daytime resting caves to embark on solitary nocturnal hunting excursions across the deep reef slopes. As dedicated carnivores, their diet centers around deep-water cuttlefish, squid, eels, snappers, and small benthic sharks.

To locate food in the pitch-black depths, the coelacanth drifts slowly along the bottom currents, often assuming a peculiar head-down vertical orientation. It utilizes a specialized electrosensory snout structure called a rostral organ to detect the faint electromagnetic fields produced by hidden prey before snapping them up with its powerful jaws.

## How does it behave?

Coelacanths are lethargic, energy-conserving swimmers that avoid vigorous tail flapping unless escaping an immediate threat. Their most spectacular behavioral feature is their locomotion; their fleshy, stalked pectoral and pelvic fins move in an alternating diagonal pattern—the exact same mechanical coordination seen in a trotting horse or lizard.

Despite their tranquil daytime aggregations inside volcanic caves, coelacanths do not exhibit complex social hierarchies or aggressive territoriality toward one another, preferring to operate as quiet, solitary wanderers in the open ocean.

## Life cycle and babies

The reproductive biology of the coelacanth is extraordinary and distinct from most marine fishes. They are ovoviviparous, meaning females retain their internally fertilized eggs inside their reproductive tract. The developing pups do not attach to a placenta; instead, they draw essential nourishment directly from massive individual yolk sacs.

Recent scientific investigations indicate that the coelacanth's gestation period is exceptionally prolonged, lasting between three to five years—the longest known pregnancy in the entire animal kingdom. When gestation concludes, the mother gives live birth to between 5 and 25 fully formed pups, each measuring around 14 inches long. The young are immediately independent and seek refuge in deep rock crevices to avoid predation.

## Why is it at risk?

The West Indian Ocean coelacanth is classified as Critically Endangered on the IUCN Red List. While their deep-water cave habitats shelter them from many direct anthropogenic impacts, they remain highly vulnerable to accidental capture as bycatch in deep-set gillnets intended for deep-water oilfish and sharks.

Because coelacanths possess incredibly slow metabolic rates, late sexual maturity (estimated around 40 to 50 years of age), and long gestation cycles, even minor population losses can have catastrophic consequences for the species' survival. Marine conservation organizations and regional governments are working diligently to establish deep-water marine protected areas and educate local fishing fleets to safely release accidentally snagged specimens.
