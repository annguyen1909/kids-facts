## What is an American bison?

The American bison is the **largest land animal in North America**. It has a big shaggy head, a tall shoulder **hump**, short curved horns, and a thick brown coat that keeps it warm.

People sometimes call bison "buffalo," but true buffalo live in Africa and Asia. The American bison is its own kind of animal, with the scientific name *Bison bison*. Bison are powerful but mostly calm plant-eaters.

## Where do American bison live?

Bison are animals of the open **grasslands and prairies** of North America. They like wide, grassy spaces with room to roam and rivers to drink from.

Long ago, enormous herds covered much of the continent. Today most bison live in **national parks, refuges, and protected herds** in the United States and Canada, such as Yellowstone.

## What do American bison eat?

Bison are **herbivores**, which means they eat plants. Their main food is **grass**, along with sedges and other low plants. A grown bison can eat many pounds of grass every single day.

Like cows, bison are **ruminants**. They swallow their food, then bring it back up to chew again as "cud," which helps them get the most from tough prairie grass.

## How do American bison live in herds?

Bison are social animals that live together in groups called **herds**. Living in a herd helps protect the calves and lets the animals watch for danger together.

During the mating season, big bulls show off and sometimes crash heads in pushing contests to win the chance to mate. The rest of the year, herds graze, rest, and move calmly across the grasslands.

## Life cycle and calves

A baby bison is called a **calf**. Calves are born in spring with bright **orange-red fur**, which is why people nickname them "red dogs." They can stand and run within hours of being born.

Calves drink their mother's milk for several months while staying close inside the herd. As they grow, their coats turn dark brown and their shoulder hump and horns slowly develop.

## Why were American bison once at risk?

Bison once numbered in the **tens of millions**, but in the 1800s they were hunted on a massive scale. Their numbers crashed until only a few hundred were left, and the species nearly disappeared forever.

Thanks to early **conservation** work, bison were saved and slowly brought back. Today they are listed as Near Threatened, and protected herds keep this iconic animal roaming the plains.

## Built for cold and dust

Bison are tough survivors. In winter, a bison swings its massive head from side to side like a **snowplow**, sweeping snow away to reach the grass underneath.

On hot days, bison **wallow** by rolling in dust and dirt. This dust bath helps clean their coats and keep biting insects away. Their thick fur and strong bodies let them handle both blizzards and heat.
