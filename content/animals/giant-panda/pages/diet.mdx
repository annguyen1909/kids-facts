## Are giant pandas carnivores or herbivores?

Giant pandas are **herbivores**—animals that eat plants. In the wild, they behave like **bamboo specialists**. Most of their day is spent finding and chewing bamboo.

Giant pandas belong to the order Carnivora (the same group as bears, cats, and dogs), but their diet is almost entirely plant-based. Their digestive system and teeth are adapted for bamboo rather than hunting large prey.

## What is bamboo?

**Bamboo** is a fast-growing plant with hollow stems and tough leaves. In giant panda habitat, bamboo grows thickly on mountain slopes and in forest understory. Pandas rely on it because it is available year-round in many forests.

They prefer tender **shoots** and fresh **leaves**, which are easier to chew and digest than older, woody stems.

## How do giant pandas eat bamboo?

Giant pandas use strong **jaws and large molars** to crush and grind bamboo. Their most famous feeding tool is the **pseudo-thumb**—an extended wrist bone that helps them grasp bamboo like a thumb and finger.

This adaptation lets a giant panda:

- Hold a bamboo stem steady
- Strip leaves efficiently
- Feed while sitting on the ground or in a tree

## Why do pandas eat so much bamboo?

Bamboo is **low in calories**, so giant pandas must eat large amounts to get enough energy. An adult panda may spend **12 to 14 hours a day** feeding.

Because feeding takes so much time, pandas move slowly and rest often. Their round body shape helps them sit comfortably while munching bamboo for hours.

## What else do giant pandas eat?

When bamboo is scarce or seasons change, giant pandas may eat small amounts of:

- **Roots and tubers**
- **Fruit**
- **Occasional small animals or carrion**

This flexibility is rare, however. Bamboo remains their essential food.

## Why does diet matter for conservation?

If bamboo forests are cut down or broken up by roads, giant pandas lose their main food source. Protecting whole forest areas—not just single bamboo patches—helps pandas find enough food every day.
