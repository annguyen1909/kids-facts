## What is a giant panda?

A giant panda is a large **black-and-white bear** from China. It has a round body, short tail, and bold patches of black fur around its eyes, ears, shoulders, and legs. Giant pandas are one of the most recognizable animals in the world.

Giant pandas belong to the **bear family Ursidae**. Their scientific name is *Ailuropoda melanoleuca*, which means "black-and-white cat-foot." Despite that name, they are bears—not cats.

## Where do giant pandas live?

Wild giant pandas live only in **central China**, mainly in mountain forests of **Sichuan, Shaanxi, and Gansu** provinces. These forests are cool, rainy, and filled with bamboo.

Pandas use **bamboo forests on mountain slopes** between about 4,000 and 10,000 feet (1,200 to 3,000 meters) above sea level. Most wild pandas today survive inside **protected reserves**.

## What do giant pandas eat?

Giant pandas are **herbivores** that eat almost nothing but **bamboo**. They munch on leaves, shoots, and stems for many hours each day—sometimes up to 14 hours.

An extra wrist bone works like a **pseudo-thumb**, helping pandas grip bamboo while they sit and feed. Because bamboo is low in calories, pandas must eat large amounts to get enough energy.

## How do giant pandas live?

Adult giant pandas usually live **alone**. Each panda keeps its own home range and marks territory with scent. The main exception is a **mother raising cubs**—she feeds, protects, and teaches them to climb and eat bamboo.

Giant pandas are also good **climbers** and sometimes rest in trees. They move slowly on the ground but can be playful, especially when rolling in snow or playing with cubs.

## Why are giant pandas at risk?

Giant pandas are listed as **[Vulnerable](/conservation-status/vulnerable)**. Their wild numbers fell for many years because forests were cut down and broken into smaller pieces, leaving pandas with less bamboo and less room to roam.

Today, **protected reserves**, forest corridors, and breeding programs have helped panda numbers slowly rise. Continued habitat protection remains essential for their long-term survival.

## Life cycle and cubs

A baby giant panda is called a **cub**. Newborns are tiny—about the size of a stick of butter—and depend completely on their mother. Cubs learn to climb trees and eat bamboo as they grow.

Mothers usually raise one or two cubs at a time in cool mountain forests.
