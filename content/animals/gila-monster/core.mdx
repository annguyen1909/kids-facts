## What is a Gila monster?

The Gila monster is a large, heavy-bodied lizard native to the arid deserts of North America. Known scientifically as *Heloderma suspectum*, it is famous for being one of the very few venomous lizards in the world.

Gila monsters are the largest lizards native to the United States. They have a stout body, a broad head, a short, thick tail, and short legs with sharp claws. Their most striking feature is their bumpy skin, decorated with bold, marble-like patterns of black and pink, orange, or yellow. These bumps are actually tiny beads of bone embedded in their skin, called **osteoderms**, which act like a suit of chainmail armor.

As reptiles, Gila monsters are cold-blooded, covered in scales, breathe air, and lay eggs on land.

## Where do Gila monsters live?

Gila monsters live in the southwestern regions of **North America**. 

Their range covers the southwestern **United States** (primarily Arizona and New Mexico, but also parts of California, Nevada, and Utah) and northwestern **Mexico** (in the state of Sonora). They make their homes in dry, arid environments, including **deserts, oak woodlands, rocky foothills, and shrublands**. 

They prefer areas with rocky crevices, sandy soil, or moist soils near streams, which they use to dig burrows or find shelter from the scorching desert heat.

## What do Gila monsters eat?

Gila monsters are **carnivores** that feed on a variety of small desert creatures and their eggs.

Their diet consists mainly of **eggs** of birds and reptiles, which they find by raiding nests. They also hunt small mammals like baby mice, rats, and rabbits, as well as small birds, frogs, and other lizards. Because they are slow-moving, they cannot chase down fast prey. Instead, they rely on finding defenseless prey, such as nests of babies or eggs, swallowing their food whole.

A Gila monster has a very slow metabolism and can store fat in its thick tail, allowing it to go months without eating after a few successful meals!

## How do Gila monsters behave?

Gila monsters are secretive animals that spend up to **90% of their lives underground** in burrows or rocky caves to stay cool and prevent water loss. They are most active in the spring when food is plentiful.

To hunt, they use their purple, forked tongues to "taste" the air. The tongue carries scent particles to a special smelling organ in the roof of their mouth, called the Jacobson's organ, allowing them to track down buried nests.

If threatened, a Gila monster will hiss loudly and open its mouth wide to show a black lining. Unlike venomous snakes, which inject venom quickly through hollow fangs, the Gila monster has venom glands in its lower jaw. When it bites, it **holds on and chews** to let the venom flow along grooved teeth into the wound. The bite is extremely painful but is rarely fatal to humans.

## Life cycle and baby Gila monsters

Female Gila monsters lay eggs during the summer. The female digs a shallow nest in sandy soil or inside an abandoned burrow, laying a clutch of **2 to 12 eggs**. After burying the eggs, she leaves, providing no protection or care.

The eggs incubate in the warm ground for about 120 to 150 days, hatching in the fall or winter. 

The baby lizards, called **hatchlings**, emerge fully formed and measure about 6 inches (15 cm) long. They have the same bright black-and-pink patterns as adults, but their colors are even more vivid. The young hatchlings are fully independent and must immediately find insects or small eggs to eat and a safe burrow to hide from predators.

## Why are Gila monsters at risk?

The Gila monster is currently classified as **Near Threatened** by the IUCN, and their populations are decreasing.

The primary threat to Gila monsters is **habitat loss** due to urban sprawl, agriculture, and road building in the desert. As deserts are paved over, they lose the rocky foothills and vegetation they need to survive. Many are also killed by cars when trying to cross roads.

Additionally, because of their unique appearance, they are sometimes captured illegally for the pet trade, although they are protected by law in both the US and Mexico. Protecting desert preserves and creating wildlife crossings can help save these ancient desert monsters.
