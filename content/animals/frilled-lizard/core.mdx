## What is a frilled lizard?

The frilled lizard is a fascinating reptile famous for its extraordinary neck collar. Known scientifically as *Chlamydosaurus kingii*, this lizard is also called the **frill-necked lizard** or the **frilled dragon**. 

Their most spectacular feature is the large, circular cape of skin surrounding their neck. Under normal conditions, this frill lies folded flat against the lizard's neck and shoulders. But when the lizard feels threatened, it opens its mouth wide, causing the ruff to fan out like a colorful umbrella! The frill is supported by long cartilaginous rods connected to the jaw bones, and it is decorated with bright orange, red, and yellow scales.

As reptiles, frilled lizards are cold-blooded, covered in scales, and are members of the dragon lizard family Agamidae.

## Where do frilled lizards live?

Frilled lizards are native to the warm, tropical regions of **northern Australia** and the southern part of the island of **New Guinea**.

They live in dry, open woodlands, sclerophyll forests, and tropical savannas. These lizards are highly **arboreal**, which means they spend about 90% of their time living in trees. They love trees with rough bark that matches their brown and grey color, allowing them to blend in perfectly and hide from predators. They only descend to the ground to forage for food, interact with other lizards, or lay eggs.

## What do frilled lizards eat?

Frilled lizards are **carnivores** (specifically insectivores) that feed on a variety of small critters. Because they spend most of their time in trees, they have a great view of the ground and will watch for prey from a high branch.

Their diet consists mainly of **insects**, including caterpillars, cicadas, beetles, ants, termites, and grasshoppers. They also eat spiders and occasionally catch small lizards or mice. When they spot an insect on the forest floor, they climb down quickly, run to grab it, and then rush back up into the safety of the trees to eat it.

## How do frilled lizards behave?

Frilled lizards are active during the day (diurnal) and spend their mornings basking on tree trunks to warm up their bodies in the sun.

They are famous for their **defensive display**. If a predator approaches, the lizard will first freeze, hoping its camouflage makes it look like a branch. If that doesn't work, the lizard will stand up, open its mouth wide, and flare out its massive neck frill. The bright yellow inside of its mouth and the colorful frill make it look much larger and scarier than it actually is! It may also hiss and lash its tail.

If the display fails to scare the predator away, the frilled lizard is capable of **bipedal running**. It stands upright on its long hind legs and runs away at high speeds, using its tail for balance, to reach the nearest tree and climb to safety.

## Life cycle and baby frilled lizards

Female frilled lizards lay their eggs during the wet season, which runs from November to February, when food is plentiful. The female climbs down from her tree and digs a shallow nest in warm, sandy soil in a sunny spot.

She lays a clutch of **8 to 23 small eggs** and carefully buries them to keep them safe from predators before returning to the trees. The eggs incubate in the warm ground for about 70 to 90 days. 

When the baby lizards (called **hatchlings**) emerge, they are fully formed and independent. Interestingly, the temperature of the nest during incubation determines whether the hatchlings are male or female—extreme temperatures produce females, while moderate temperatures produce males. The babies have small, fully functional neck frills and can immediately run and hunt for small insects.

## Why are frilled lizards at risk?

Globally, frilled lizards are classified as **Least Concern** by the IUCN, meaning they are not currently at risk of extinction. They have large populations and a wide distribution in the wild.

However, they do face threats in some areas. Their forest habitats are sometimes cleared for farming, cattle grazing, and urban development. They are also preyed upon by introduced predators, such as feral cats, dogs, and red foxes. Bushfires during the dry season can also destroy the trees they rely on for shelter and food.

In Australia, frilled lizards are protected by law, meaning they cannot be collected from the wild or exported without a permit.
