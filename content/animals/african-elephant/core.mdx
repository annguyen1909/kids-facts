## What is an African elephant?

An African elephant is the **largest land animal** alive today. It has a long trunk, huge ears, and a strong body built for moving across big landscapes. African elephants use their trunks for smelling, touching, drinking, and gathering food.

Their scientific name is *Loxodonta africana*. They belong to the elephant family **Elephantidae**.

## Where do African elephants live?

African elephants live in habitats that give them plants to eat and room to travel. They are found across **Africa** in countries such as Kenya, Botswana, and Zimbabwe.

Some groups live in **savannas** and grasslands. Others use forests and wetland areas. Elephant families may cross huge areas while searching for water, especially in dry seasons. Explore more [savanna animals](/habitats/savanna).

## What do African elephants eat?

African elephants are **herbivores** that spend many hours feeding each day. They eat grasses, leaves, bark, and fruit. Their trunks help them grab plants and bring food to their mouths.

An adult elephant may eat hundreds of pounds of plants every day. Water holes and seasonal rains shape where they feed and rest.

## How do elephant families live?

Elephants live in close **herds**, usually led by an experienced female called a **matriarch**. Adults protect calves and help them learn where to find food and water.

Elephants communicate with body language, touch, and low rumbles that can travel through the ground. These signals help the herd stay together across open land. Read more about [social animals](/topics/social-animals).

## Life cycle and calves

A baby elephant is called a **calf**. Calves are born after about 22 months—the longest pregnancy of any land mammal. Mothers and the herd protect and teach calves for years.

Young elephants learn by watching adults use their trunks, find water, and read danger signals from the group.

## Why are African elephants at risk?

African elephants are listed as **Endangered**. Their wild numbers have fallen because of habitat loss, poaching, and conflict with people.

Protected parks, anti-poaching patrols, and community conservation programs help the remaining herds survive. Saving elephant habitat also protects many other savanna species.
