## What is a ring-tailed lemur?

A ring-tailed lemur is a **primate** about the size of a house cat. It has soft gray fur, a white face with dark patches around its bright orange eyes, and a famous **black-and-white striped tail** that is longer than its body.

Lemurs are special **primates** that live only on the island of Madagascar. The ring-tailed lemur's scientific name is *Lemur catta*, and it is the most easily recognized of all the lemurs.

## Where do ring-tailed lemurs live?

Ring-tailed lemurs live **only on Madagascar**, a large island off the coast of Africa. They are found in the dry forests, gallery forests, and spiny scrublands of the southern and southwestern parts of the island.

Unlike many tree-dwelling lemurs, ring-tailed lemurs spend a lot of time **on the ground**, walking between trees with their striped tails held high.

## What do ring-tailed lemurs eat?

Ring-tailed lemurs are **omnivores**, which means they eat both plants and small animals. Most of their food is **fruit and leaves**, and they especially love the fruit of the tamarind tree.

They also nibble flowers, drink sap and nectar, and sometimes snack on insects. Lemurs use their nimble hands to pick and hold their food while they eat.

## How do lemur troops live together?

Ring-tailed lemurs are very social and live in groups called **troops**, which can have up to about 30 members. Each troop is led by a **top female**, who gets first pick of food and the best spots to rest.

Troop members stay in touch with calls, scents, and their high striped tails. Living together helps them watch for danger and keep their young ones safe.

## Life cycle and babies

A baby lemur is called an **infant**. Newborns are tiny and cling tightly to their mother's belly. As they grow stronger, they ride on her **back** like a little jockey.

Mothers care for their infants closely, and other troop members often help watch over them. Young lemurs slowly learn to climb, leap, and find food by copying the adults around them.

## Why are ring-tailed lemurs at risk?

Ring-tailed lemurs are listed as **Endangered**, and their numbers are still **decreasing**. Because they live only on Madagascar, problems on the island affect the whole species.

As forests are cleared for farms and firewood, lemurs lose their homes. Some are also hunted or taken for the pet trade. Protected reserves on Madagascar help keep wild troops safe.

## Sunbathing and stink fights

Ring-tailed lemurs do some very funny-looking things. On cool mornings, a whole troop will sit up, stretch their arms wide, and **sunbathe** to warm their bellies in the sun.

Males also have "**stink fights**." They rub smelly scent from special glands onto their tails, then wave the tails at rivals to see who smells the strongest. The loser usually backs away.
