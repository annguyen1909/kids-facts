## What is an electric eel?

An electric eel is one of the most unique creatures in the animal kingdom. Despite their snake-like bodies and their common name, they are not true eels—they are actually a type of **knifefish** related to catfish and carp.

Electric eels are famous for their ability to generate **powerful electric shocks**. They have long, cylindrical, scale-free bodies that are covered in a slimy protective mucus. Nearly four-fifths of their body is filled with specialized organs that produce electricity, leaving only the front part for their vital organs!

Their scientific name is *Electrophorus electricus*. As fish, they belong to the class Actinopterygii, breathe air, and have gills, although their gills are not their main source of oxygen. They can grow up to **8 feet (2.5 meters) long** and weigh over 40 pounds (20 kg).

## Where do electric eels live?

Electric eels are native to the freshwater basins of **South America**. They live in the Amazon and Orinoco River basins, in countries like Brazil, Colombia, Venezuela, Peru, and Guyana.

They prefer **slow-moving, quiet rivers, muddy swamps, marshes, and flooded forests**. These waters are often very dark, muddy, and contain very little dissolved oxygen.

Because of the low oxygen in their habitat, electric eels have adapted to be **air-breathers**. They must rise to the surface of the water every 10 minutes to gulp air. They absorb oxygen through the sensitive, blood-vessel-rich skin inside their mouths.

## What do electric eels eat?

Electric eels are strict [carnivores](/diets/carnivore) and powerful hunters. They eat a variety of aquatic animals, including **fish, frogs, crabs, shrimp, and occasionally small birds and mammals** that fall into the water.

They do not have teeth. Instead, they hunt by sending out a high-voltage electrical discharge. When the shock hits a nearby animal, it causes the prey's muscles to spasm, **paralyzing it instantly**. 

Once the prey is stunned, the electric eel swims up, opens its mouth wide, and sucks the helpless animal in whole. They can also use their electricity to double-shock hiding prey, causing the prey to twitch and reveal its location in the muddy water.

## How do electric eels behave?

Electric eels are solitary, nocturnal animals that spend the day resting in dark underwater crevices and hunt at night.

Because they live in murky water and have very poor eyesight, they use electricity like radar. They emit constant, **low-voltage electric pulses** (about 10 volts) to map their surroundings, navigate, and locate prey in the dark. This is called electroreception.

When they feel threatened or spot a large predator, they generate their famous **high-voltage shock** (up to 860 volts) for self-defense. To protect themselves from their own shocks, electric eels have thick, insulated skin that prevents the current from damaging their brains and hearts.

## Life cycle and baby eels

Electric eels reproduce during the dry season. The male builds a unique **nest out of his own thick saliva (spit)** on the river bottom. The female then lays up to **17,000 eggs** inside this foam nest, which the male guards fiercely.

The eggs hatch in about a week into tiny larvae. The baby grubs feed on the foam nest and small water insects, growing rapidly. 

As they grow, they develop their electricity-generating organs. Young electric eels have a yellowish color that helps them blend in with rotting leaves on the river bed. They grow into juveniles and eventually leave the nest to start their independent, solitary lives.

## Why are electric eels at risk?

Currently, electric eels are listed as Least Concern, and their populations are healthy and stable in the wild. Because they can deliver painful shocks, humans and larger animals generally leave them alone.

However, they face threats from **habitat degradation**. Pollution from gold mining, logging, and agricultural runoff can contaminate their river habitats and kill the fish they rely on for food. 

Building large dams in the Amazon can also block their movements and change the flow of the rivers they live in. Keeping the Amazon and Orinoco rivers clean and free-flowing is vital for protecting the electric eel's unique environment.
