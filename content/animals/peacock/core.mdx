## What is a peacock?

A peacock is a large, colorful bird belonging to the pheasant family Phasianidae. They are famous all over the world for the male's **spectacular tail display**, featuring a huge fan of feathers decorated with colorful eyespots.

While we usually call these birds "peacocks," that name actually only applies to the males! The females are called **peahens**, the babies are **peachicks**, and as a group, they are called **peafowl**. Peacocks are bright blue and green, while peahens are mostly brown and grey so they can blend in with the ground when nesting.

Their scientific name is *Pavo cristatus*, which refers specifically to the Indian Peafowl, the most common and widely recognized species. As birds, they are warm-blooded, have feathers, lay eggs, and have wings.

## Where do peacocks live?

Peacocks are native to the warm, tropical woodlands of the **Indian subcontinent**, which includes India, Sri Lanka, Nepal, and Bangladesh. 

They prefer habitats like **deciduous forests, scrublands, grasslands, and agricultural fields** near rivers or streams. They love areas with tall trees where they can fly up to sleep at night, and open ground where they can search for food during the day.

Because of their incredible beauty, peacocks have been introduced by humans to many other countries, including the United States, Australia, and parts of Europe, where they are kept in parks, estates, and gardens.

## What do peacocks eat?

Peacocks are **omnivores** with a very diverse diet. They forage on the ground, scratching at the soil with their strong feet to find food, much like a domestic chicken.

Their diet consists of seeds, grains, berries, fruits, and flower buds. However, they also hunt and eat small creatures, including insects, worms, centipedes, frogs, and lizards. 

Peacocks are especially famous in India for **hunting and eating snakes**, including young, venomous cobras! Because they keep snake populations down, they are welcomed by people living in rural villages and farms. They get most of their water from the morning dew and the food they eat.

## How do peacocks behave?

Peacocks are active during the day and spend their time walking and foraging in small groups called parties. When night falls, they fly up into the branches of **tall forest trees** to roost together, keeping safe from predators like leopards and tigers.

The most famous behavior of the peacock is his **mating display**. During the breeding season, the male lifts his train of tail feathers into a giant, upright fan. He shakes his feathers rapidly, creating a shimmering effect and a rustling sound to attract the attention of peahens.

They are also very vocal birds. During the breeding season, peacocks make loud, high-pitched screeching calls that sound like a loud "may-awe!" to establish their territory and call to peahens.

## Life cycle and baby peacocks

A peahen digs a shallow nest in the ground, hidden under thick grass or bushes. She lays a clutch of **4 to 8 pale green or white eggs** and sits on them for about 28 days to keep them warm until they hatch.

Baby peafowl, called **peachicks**, are born covered in fluffy brown down. Remarkably, peachicks are able to walk, run, and even fly short distances just a few days after hatching! 

They follow their mother closely, learning how to scratch the ground for seeds and insects. The young peachicks grow quickly. Male peachicks do not start to grow their famous, long tail feathers until they are about **three years old**.

## Why are peacocks at risk?

Indian Peacocks are listed as Least Concern, and their wild populations are healthy and stable. In India, they are protected by strict laws and are honored as the **National Bird of India**.

However, they still face local threats from **habitat loss** as forests are cleared for towns and farms. They also face conflict with farmers because they sometimes wander into agricultural fields and eat crops, leading to accidental poisoning from chemical pesticides.

In some areas, they are hunted illegally for their feathers and meat. Fortunately, because peacocks naturally shed their beautiful tail feathers every year after the breeding season, humans can collect and sell their feathers without harming the birds.
