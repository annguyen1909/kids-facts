## What is an Atlantic puffin?

The Atlantic puffin is a small, charismatic seabird famous for its colorful appearance and cute, waddling walk. Known scientifically as *Fratercula arctica*, it is also nicknamed the **"sea parrot"** or the **"clown of the ocean"** because of its bright orange beak and white face with black markings around its eyes.

Puffins are about the size of a pigeon. They have waterproof black-and-white feathers that make them look like they are wearing a tuxedo. Their most famous feature is their large, triangular, bright orange beak. Interestingly, this bill is only colorful during the spring and summer breeding season. In the winter, the puffin sheds the outer parts of its beak, leaving it smaller and a dull grey color.

As seabirds, puffins are warm-blooded, covered in feathers, and belong to the auk family Alcidae.

## Where do Atlantic puffins live?

As their name suggests, Atlantic puffins live in the cold waters of the **North Atlantic Ocean**. 

They spend most of their lives—about 8 months of the year—out on the open ocean, far away from land. They sleep, swim, and feed on the water's surface. However, in the spring, millions of puffins return to land to breed. They build nests on **steep, grassy clifftops, rocky islands, and headlands** in northern countries like Iceland, Norway, Greenland, Canada, the United Kingdom, and the northeastern United States. Iceland is the puffin capital of the world, where more than half of the world's population nests every summer!

## What do Atlantic puffins eat?

Atlantic puffins are **carnivores** that hunt for marine life in the ocean. Their diet consists almost entirely of **small fish**.

Their favorite prey includes sand eels, capelin, sprats, and young herring. They also eat small crustaceans like krill and shrimp. When hunting, puffins dive from the air or the surface of the water, swimming down to catch fish. They can stay submerged for up to a minute, using their webbed feet to steer and their wings to fly through the water.

## How do Atlantic puffins behave?

Atlantic puffins are excellent flyers and swimmers, but they are rather clumsy on land, where they waddle slowly and sometimes crash-land. 

During the winter, puffins are solitary animals, living alone on the cold ocean. But in summer, they become highly social and gather in massive nesting colonies containing thousands of birds. 

To communicate, puffins rub their colorful bills together in a behavior called **"billing."** This is a sign of affection between mated partners. They also make deep, growling noises from inside their burrows that sound like a chainsaw. When defending their nests, they will stomp their feet, open their wings, and gape their beaks to scare off rivals.

## Life cycle and baby Atlantic puffins

Instead of building nests out of twigs in trees, puffins use their strong webbed feet and sharp claws to dig **underground burrows** in the grassy cliff soil. These burrows can be up to 3 feet (1 meter) deep, protecting them from cold weather and predators.

A puffin pair mates for life and raises a single egg each year. Both parents take turns sitting on the egg for about 39 to 45 days. 

When the baby hatches, it is called a **puffling**! It is covered in fluffy black down. The parents fly back and forth to the ocean to bring the puffling fresh fish. Thanks to tiny backward-pointing spines on the roof of their mouths, adult puffins can carry up to 60 small fish in their beaks at once! The puffling stays safe inside its dark burrow for about 6 weeks. When it is ready, it flies out to sea under the cover of night to avoid predators.

## Why are Atlantic puffins at risk?

The Atlantic puffin is currently listed as **Vulnerable** to extinction because their populations have been declining rapidly.

The main threat to puffins is **overfishing**, which reduces the number of small fish available for them to eat and feed their babies. **Climate change** is also warming the oceans, causing fish to move to colder waters further north, making it harder for puffins to find food near their colonies.

Pollution is another major problem. Oil spills can coat their feathers, destroying their waterproofing and causing them to freeze. They also face threats from introduced predators like rats and cats on nesting islands, which eat eggs and pufflings. Conservation groups are working hard to protect nesting islands and regulate fishing to help these beloved birds survive.
