## What is a Luna moth?

The Luna moth is one of the largest and most spectacular insects in North America. Known scientifically as *Actias luna*, it is famous for its massive size, bright lime-green wings, and long, swallowtail-like tails.

Adult Luna moths have a wingspan of up to 4.5 inches (11.5 cm). Their wings are a beautiful pale green color with a purple or brown leading edge. They also feature four prominent, eye-like spots (eyespots) on their wings, which help scare off predators. Their most distinctive feature is the pair of long, twisted tails extending from their hindwings. 

As insects, Luna moths have six legs, three body sections (head, thorax, and abdomen), and undergo a complete metamorphosis. They belong to the giant silkworm moth family Saturniidae.

## Where do Luna moths live?

Luna moths are native only to **North America**. They live in deciduous hardwood forests, woodlands, and suburban areas throughout eastern Canada, down through the eastern half of the United States, and into parts of northern Mexico.

Because they are active only at night, they are rarely seen by humans during the day. They spend daylight hours clinging to the undersides of leaves or tree trunks, where their green color and leaf-like wing shapes make them almost completely invisible to predators.

## What do Luna moths eat?

The diet of a Luna moth depends entirely on its life stage—and it is one of the most surprising things about them!

As adults, **Luna moths do not eat at all**. In fact, they do not even have mouths, tongues, or digestive tracts! Their only purpose as adults is to fly, mate, and lay eggs, using energy stored from when they were caterpillars.

However, as **caterpillars**, they are very hungry herbivores. They eat leaves from deciduous trees, especially paper birch, sweetgum, American persimmon, walnut, hickory, and sumac. A caterpillar spends its entire life eating leaves, growing up to 3 inches long before spinning its cocoon.

## How do Luna moths behave?

Luna moths are strictly **nocturnal**, meaning they are active only at night. During the day, they rest quietly, using their camouflage to blend in with foliage.

Because they are active in the dark, they are frequent targets for bats. To defend themselves, Luna moths have a brilliant adaptation: their **long, twisted wing tails**. When the moth flies, the tails spin behind it. This spinning movement disrupts the echolocation (sonar) signals sent out by hunting bats, causing the bats to attack the tails rather than the moth's body, allowing the moth to escape.

To find mates, female Luna moths sit quietly and release chemical scents called pheromones. Male Luna moths use their large, feathery antennae to smell these pheromones, allowing them to fly and find females from over a mile away!

## Life cycle and baby Luna moths

A female Luna moth lays **200 to 400 small eggs** on the undersides of leaves. The eggs hatch in about 10 to 14 days, revealing tiny, bright green caterpillars.

The caterpillars spend the next 3 to 4 weeks eating leaf after leaf. As they grow, they shed their skin several times. When it is time to transform, the caterpillar climbs down to the forest floor and spins a cocoon made of tough silk, wrapping it inside a fallen leaf to hide it.

Inside the cocoon, the caterpillar undergoes metamorphosis. In northern regions, they spend the entire winter inside their cocoon and emerge in spring. When the adult moth emerges, it pumps fluid into its wings to expand them, and flies off into the night.

## Why are Luna moths at risk?

The Luna moth is listed as a species of **Least Concern** because they have a wide distribution and healthy wild populations. 

However, they face local threats from **habitat loss** as deciduous forests are cleared for housing and roads. Another major threat is **pesticide use**, which can kill both caterpillars and adult moths.

**Light pollution** is also a problem. Like many moths, Luna moths are attracted to bright streetlights and porch lights. When they hover around artificial lights all night, they waste their precious, limited energy and are easily caught by predators like bats and birds. Keeping outdoor lights off at night during spring and summer can help protect these beautiful night flyers.
