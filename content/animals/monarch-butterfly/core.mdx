## What is a monarch butterfly?

A monarch butterfly is a large, colorful insect belonging to the family Nymphalidae. It is famous for its **bright orange wings with black veins and white spots** along the edges. The bright colors serve as a warning to predators that the butterfly is toxic and tastes terrible.

Their scientific name is *Danaus plexippus*. As insects, they have six legs, a body divided into three parts (head, thorax, and abdomen), and a pair of antennae.

## Where do monarch butterflies live?

Monarch butterflies are found primarily in **North America**, but they also live in Central America, South America, Australia, New Zealand, and parts of Europe. They prefer open habitats such as **meadows, prairies, fields, and gardens** where wildflowers are abundant.

During the cold winter months, monarchs migrate to very specific locations. Monarchs in eastern North America fly to the high-altitude fir forests of central Mexico, while western monarchs cluster in warm eucalyptus groves along the coast of California.

## What do monarch butterflies eat?

A monarch's diet changes completely as it grows. **Monarch caterpillars eat only milkweed leaves**. They are eating machines, consuming vast amounts of milkweed to fuel their rapid growth.

Adult monarch butterflies, on the other hand, drink **liquid nectar** from various wildflowers, including milkweed, clover, and goldenrod. They use a straw-like mouthpart called a **proboscis** to sip the sweet nectar.

## Migration size and scale

Monarchs are famous for their **incredible long-distance migration**. Every autumn, millions of North American monarchs fly south to escape the freezing winter. Some butterflies travel **up to 3,000 miles (4,800 km)** to reach their winter homes in Mexico or California.

What makes this journey even more amazing is that the butterflies flying south have never been there before! They use the angle of the sun and the Earth's magnetic fields to navigate. In the spring, they head back north, breeding along the way.

## Life cycle and transformation

Like all butterflies, the monarch undergoes a complete change called **metamorphosis**. This journey has four distinct stages:
1. **The Egg**: A female monarch lays a tiny egg on a milkweed leaf.
2. **The Caterpillar**: The egg hatches into a striped caterpillar. It grows rapidly, shedding its skin five times.
3. **The Chrysalis**: The caterpillar spins a silk pad, hangs upside down, and forms a shiny green chrysalis with golden dots.
4. **The Butterfly**: Inside the chrysalis, the body transforms. After 10 to 14 days, a beautiful adult butterfly emerges.

## How do monarchs protect themselves?

Monarchs have a unique defense system: they are **poisonous to predators**. When monarch caterpillars eat milkweed, they absorb natural toxins from the plant's sap. 

These toxins remain in their bodies even after they become butterflies. If a bird tries to eat a monarch, it gets sick and learns to avoid butterflies with bright orange and black patterns. Another butterfly, the **Viceroy**, mimics the monarch's pattern to trick birds into leaving it alone too.

## Why are monarch butterflies at risk?

Wild monarch populations have declined significantly in recent years. Their main threat is **habitat loss**, particularly the disappearance of milkweed plants due to farming and pesticide use. 

Climate change also affects their migration schedules and winter forests. Conservation groups encourage people to plant native milkweed and nectar flowers in their backyards to create "monarch waystations" and help these beautiful insects survive.
