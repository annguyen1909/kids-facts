## Are tigers carnivores?

Yes. Tigers are **[carnivores](/diets/carnivore)**—animals that eat meat. They belong to the order Carnivora and the cat family [Felidae](/families/felidae), the same group as lions and leopards.

An adult tiger needs a lot of meat to fuel its large, muscular body. In the wild, almost everything a tiger eats comes from **hunting live prey**.

## What animals do tigers hunt?

Tigers are **apex predators**—top hunters in their habitat. Common prey includes:

- **Deer** — such as sambar and chital in India
- **Wild pigs**
- **Buffalo** and **gaur** (large wild cattle)
- **Antelope** and other hoofed mammals

Tigers choose prey they can catch safely. They often target animals that are young, old, or injured.

## How do tigers hunt?

Tigers hunt **alone** and mostly at **dawn, dusk, and night**. Their hunting style is **ambush**, not long-distance chase:

1. **Stalk** — move silently through grass or forest cover
2. **Pounce** — burst forward in a short, powerful attack
3. **Bite** — use strong jaws to grab the neck or throat
4. **Drag** — pull the kill into cover to feed safely

Stripes break up a tiger's outline in shadows, helping it get close before attacking.

## How much can a tiger eat?

Tigers do not need to eat every day. After a successful hunt, a tiger may eat a large meal and then **rest for days**. A very hungry tiger can consume dozens of pounds of meat in one sitting.

Mother tigers with cubs hunt more often to feed their growing young.

## What happens when prey is scarce?

When forests shrink or prey animals decline, tigers struggle to find enough food. They may:

- Travel farther inside their home range
- Hunt smaller animals when large prey is unavailable
- Come closer to farms and villages—creating danger for people and livestock

Protecting **whole habitats**—forests plus prey populations—helps tigers find food naturally.
