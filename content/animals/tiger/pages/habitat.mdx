## What is a tiger habitat?

A **habitat** is the natural home where an animal finds food, water, shelter, and space to raise young. For tigers, good habitats include **forests**, **grasslands**, **wetlands**, and **mangrove swamps** with plenty of prey and places to hide.

Tigers are **adaptable**. Different subspecies use different landscapes—from tropical jungles in India to cold forests in Russia.

## Where do tigers live in the wild?

Today, wild tigers survive in **patches of Asia**. Important range countries include:

- **India** — home to most of the world's wild tigers, in reserves such as Bandhavgarh and Tadoba
- **Nepal** and **Bangladesh** — forest and grassland parks
- **Russia** — cold forests where Amur (Siberian) tigers live
- **China**, **Thailand**, **Malaysia**, and **Indonesia** — smaller but vital populations

A century ago, tigers ranged across much of Asia. Their range is much smaller now because forests were cleared and prey animals declined.

## What do tigers need in their habitat?

A healthy tiger habitat must provide:

- **Cover** — tall grass, bushes, and trees for stalking prey
- **Prey animals** — deer, wild pigs, buffalo, and antelope
- **Water** — rivers, lakes, and streams for drinking and cooling off
- **Large connected areas** — adult tigers need wide home ranges to hunt

Tigers mark their territory with scent so other tigers know where boundaries are.

## Different habitats for different tigers

Not all tigers live in the same kind of place:

- **Bengal tigers** often use dry deciduous forests and grasslands in India
- **Amur tigers** survive snowy winters in Russian and Chinese forests
- **Sumatran tigers** live in dense tropical rainforests
- **Sundarbans tigers** swim through mangrove swamps in Bangladesh and India

Stripes help tigers blend into **dappled light and shadows** in all these habitats.

## Why is habitat loss a problem?

Tigers are **Endangered**. Their numbers fell sharply when forests were cut for farms, roads, and towns. Fewer forests mean:

- Less prey for tigers to hunt
- More conflict with people and livestock
- Smaller, isolated groups that struggle to breed

Protected reserves, anti-poaching patrols, and habitat corridors help the remaining wild tigers survive.
