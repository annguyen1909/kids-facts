## Why tiger behavior is special

Tigers are the **largest wild cats**, but they do not live in big social groups like lions. Most of their behavior is built around **solitary hunting**, **territory**, and **quiet communication**.

Understanding how tigers behave helps explain why they need large, connected forests—and why mothers invest so much time in raising cubs.

## Do tigers live alone?

Yes. **Adult tigers are mostly solitary.** Each tiger keeps its own **home range**—an area large enough to find prey and water. They defend this space with **scent marks**, scratch marks on trees, and loud roars.

Tigers meet others mainly during **mating season**. Otherwise, seeing two adult tigers together is unusual unless one is a mother with cubs.

## When are tigers active?

Tigers are often most active at **dawn, dusk, and night**. During these hours they:

- Stalk prey in cooler temperatures
- Travel along forest paths and riverbanks
- Drink and sometimes swim
- Mark territory boundaries

During hot afternoons, tigers rest in shade—often near water.

## How do tigers hunt and communicate?

Tigers combine **stealth** with **power**:

- **Silent stalking** through tall grass and forest cover
- **Night vision** that helps them see in low light
- **Roars and chuffs** to communicate over long distances
- **Scent marking** to warn other tigers to stay away

A tiger's roar can carry for miles. Chuffing—a soft snort—is often used between a mother and her cubs.

## Can tigers swim?

Unlike many cats, tigers are **excellent swimmers**. In mangrove swamps and forest reserves, they may:

- Cross rivers to reach new hunting areas
- Soak in water to cool down on hot days
- Chase prey near water edges

The Sundarbans region is famous for tigers that move through tidal mangrove channels.

## How do mothers raise cubs?

A mother tiger gives birth to **two to four cubs** in a hidden den—often in thick vegetation or a rocky shelter. Cubs are born **blind and helpless**. The mother:

- Nurses them for several months
- Moves them to new dens if danger appears
- Brings them meat as they grow
- Teaches them to stalk, pounce, and kill prey

Cubs stay with their mother for **about two years**. Then they leave to find their own territory.

## Why does behavior matter for conservation?

Solitary tigers need **large, quiet forests** with enough prey. When roads and farms break habitats into small pieces, tigers cannot hunt safely or meet mates easily.

Protecting behavior—giving tigers space to roam, hunt, and raise cubs—is just as important as protecting individual animals.
