## What is a tiger?

A tiger is the **largest wild cat** in the world. It has a muscular body, powerful legs, sharp teeth, and famous **orange fur with black stripes**. No two tigers have exactly the same stripe pattern.

Tigers belong to the cat family [Felidae](/families/felidae), in the same genus as lions and leopards. Their scientific name is *Panthera tigris*.

## Where do tigers live?

Wild tigers live in **Asia**—from India and Nepal to Russia and parts of Southeast Asia. They use tropical forests, grasslands, mangrove swamps, and cool northern forests depending on the subspecies.

Most tigers today survive inside **protected reserves** where forests and prey animals are still available.

## What do tigers eat?

Tigers are [carnivores](/diets/carnivore). They hunt large animals such as deer, wild pigs, and buffalo. A tiger usually hunts **alone**, stalking through cover before a short, powerful attack.

After a large kill, a tiger may rest and feed for several days before hunting again.

## How do tigers live?

Unlike lions, tigers are **mostly solitary**. Each adult keeps its own home range and marks territory with scent. The main exception is a **mother raising cubs**—she feeds, protects, and teaches them to hunt.

Tigers are also strong **swimmers** and often enter rivers and lakes to cool off.

## Why are tigers at risk?

Tigers are listed as **Endangered**. Their wild numbers fell sharply during the 1900s because of habitat loss, fewer prey animals, and illegal hunting.

Today, conservation programs, anti-poaching patrols, and protected reserves help the remaining wild populations survive.

## Life cycle and cubs

A baby tiger is called a **cub**. Cubs are born blind and stay in a hidden den while the mother hunts nearby. They learn to stalk, swim, and hunt over about two years before living on their own.

Female tigers raise cubs alone, teaching them the skills needed to survive in forests and grasslands.
