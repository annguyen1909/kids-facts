## What is a dragonfly?

A dragonfly is a fascinating, ancient insect belonging to the order Odonata. They have been flying on Earth for over **300 million years**, which means their ancestors were buzzing around long before the dinosaurs appeared!

Dragonflies are easily recognized by their long, slender bodies, two pairs of strong transparent wings, and **giant compound eyes** that cover almost their entire head. They come in a spectacular rainbow of colors, including metallic blues, bright greens, and vibrant reds.

Their scientific name is *Anax imperator*, which refers specifically to the Emperor Dragonfly, one of the largest and most colorful dragonfly species. As insects, they have a hard exoskeleton, six legs, and three main body parts: the head, thorax, and abdomen.

## Where do dragonflies live?

Dragonflies are found on every continent in the world except Antarctica. Because their life cycle is closely tied to water, they live near **freshwater wetlands, ponds, marshes, lakes, and slow-moving streams**.

They prefer sunny areas with plenty of bankside vegetation, such as reeds, rushes, and water lilies. These plants give them places to perch, hide from predators, and lay their eggs. 

During the warm summer months, dragonflies can also be seen flying over meadows and gardens, sometimes quite far from water, as they search for insects to eat. However, they must always return to the water to mate and lay their eggs.

## What do dragonflies eat?

Dragonflies are fierce predators and strict [carnivores](/diets/carnivore). They are incredibly beneficial to humans because they consume huge numbers of pest insects, especially **mosquitoes, flies, gnats, and midges**. 

A single dragonfly can eat **hundreds of mosquitoes every day**! They are expert aerial hunters, catching their prey mid-air. They fly with their six legs tucked together like a basket to scoop up flying insects right out of the sky.

Dragonflies have a success rate of about **95% when hunting**, making them some of the most successful predators in the animal kingdom. They are also known to eat other, smaller dragonflies and damselflies when food is scarce.

## How do dragonflies behave?

Dragonflies are famous for their **incredible flying skills**. They can fly forward at speeds of up to 30 miles per hour (48 km/h), hover in one place like a helicopter, fly backwards, and even fly upside down! They can make sharp turns in a split second because they can move each of their four wings independently.

They are highly territorial insects. Males will patrol a specific stretch of water, flying back and forth to defend their territory from other males and search for females.

A dragonfly's vision is outstanding. Their two giant compound eyes contain up to **30,000 individual lenses** each, giving them nearly a 360-degree view of the world. This helps them spot tiny flying prey and avoid predators like birds and frogs.

## Life cycle and baby dragonflies

A dragonfly's life is split into three main stages: egg, nymph, and adult. The female lays her eggs on aquatic plants or directly into the water. After a week or two, the eggs hatch into baby dragonflies called **nymphs**.

Nymphs look very different from adults. They do not have wings, and they live underwater. Nymphs are aggressive predators, using a special mask-like jaw that shoots out to catch tadpoles, small fish, and other water insects.

The nymph stage is the longest part of a dragonfly's life, lasting from **a few months to up to five years**! When it is ready to become an adult, the nymph climbs out of the water onto a plant stem, sheds its hard outer skin one last time, expands its new wings, and flies away as an adult dragonfly.

## Why are dragonflies at risk?

Although most dragonfly species are common and listed as Least Concern, they are highly sensitive to changes in their environment.

The biggest threat to dragonflies is the **loss of wetland habitats**. When swamps, marshes, and ponds are drained or filled in for buildings and farming, dragonflies lose the water they need to reproduce. 

They are also threatened by **water pollution**. Chemicals and pesticides running into ponds can kill the aquatic nymphs directly or destroy the water plants they need to hide in. Protecting clean water sources is essential for keeping dragonflies healthy and buzzing in our skies.
