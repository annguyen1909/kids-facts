## What is a leafcutter ant?

A leafcutter ant is a fascinating social insect belonging to the family Formicidae. They are famous for being **nature's agricultural experts**, known for cutting up green leaves and carrying them back to their underground nests.

These ants are divided into different sizes, known as **castes**, and each size has a specific job in the colony. Minis are tiny ants that tend the gardens; media are leaf cutters that cut and carry leaves; and soldiers are massive ants with sharp jaws that protect the nest from invaders.

Their scientific name is *Atta cephalotes*. As insects, they have six legs, a hard outer exoskeleton, and a body divided into three parts: head, thorax, and abdomen.

## Where do leafcutter ants live?

Leafcutter ants live in the warm, humid regions of **Central and South America**, as well as parts of Mexico. They are primarily found in **tropical rainforests, dry forests, and savannas**.

They build massive underground nests that can extend up to **30 feet (9 meters) wide** and go down 20 feet into the ground. These nests are incredibly complex, containing thousands of chambers connected by tunnels. 

Some chambers are used as nurseries for baby ants, others are massive fungus gardens, and some are even used as garbage dumps! A colony is so large it can contain up to **5 million individual ants** living together.

## What do leafcutter ants eat?

Even though they cut and collect green leaves, leafcutter ants do not actually eat the leaves! Instead, they are **fungus farmers**, and they eat a very specific type of fungus that they grow themselves.

Back in their nests, the worker ants chew the leaves into a wet paste and mix it with saliva. They use this leaf paste as compost to grow a **special white fungus** inside underground chambers. 

The fungus breaks down the tough leaves and grows tiny, nutritious knobs that the ants harvest and eat. It is a perfect partnership (symbiosis)—the ants feed the fungus leaves, and the fungus feeds the ants!

## How do leafcutter ants behave?

Leafcutter ants are incredibly social and highly organized. They work together along **busy forest trails**, marching in long lines. A worker ant carrying a leaf looks like a tiny green sail, which is why they are sometimes called "parasol ants."

To navigate, they mark their trails with chemical scents called **pheromones**. Other ants follow these trails to find the best trees to cut. 

If a leaf is too large, the ant will vibrate its body to make a buzzing sound. This vibration travels through the leaf, signaling nearby worker ants to come and help chop it up. They are remarkably strong, capable of carrying objects **50 times their own body weight**!

## Life cycle and baby ants

A leafcutter ant colony revolves around a single queen, who can live for **up to 20 years** and lays all the eggs. The life cycle has four stages: egg, larva, pupa, and adult.

The queen lays tiny eggs, which hatch into legless, white **larvae**. The larvae are completely helpless and must be fed and groomed constantly by the tiny gardener ants. The larvae eat the nutritious fungus grown in the nest.

Eventually, the larva spins a cocoon and enters the **pupa** stage, where its body transforms. After a few weeks, a brand-new adult ant emerges. When new queens grow wings and fly away to start their own nests, they carry a **tiny pocket of starter fungus** in their mouths to start a new farm.

## Why are leafcutter ants at risk?

Leafcutter ants are very common and listed as Least Concern. They play an important role in the rainforest by aerating the soil with their tunnels and recycling nutrients from leaves.

However, they can face threats from **habitat destruction** when tropical rainforests are cleared for farming or roads. When trees are cut down, the ants lose their sources of leaves. 

Because they can strip entire trees of leaves in a single night, farmers sometimes view them as pests and use **chemical pesticides** to destroy their colonies. Helping farmers find natural ways to protect crops while preserving the ants' wild habitats is important for rainforest conservation.
