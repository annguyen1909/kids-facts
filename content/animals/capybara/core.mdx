## What is a capybara?

A capybara is the **world's largest rodent**—a mammal more closely related to guinea pigs than to anything that looks like it. An adult weighs up to 146 pounds (66 kg), making it heavier than a large dog.

Its scientific name, *Hydrochoerus hydrochaeris*, means "water pig" in Greek. Capybaras belong to the family [Caviidae](/families/caviidae). Explore more [South American animals](/continents/south-america).

## Where do capybaras live?

Capybaras are found across **South America**, from Colombia and Venezuela in the north to Argentina in the south. They are never far from water—rivers, lakes, marshes, and flooded savannas are their home.

During the dry season, capybara herds gather around shrinking water sources, sometimes forming groups of over 100. When the rains return, they spread out across [wetland habitats](/habitats/wetland) and grasslands.

## What do capybaras eat?

Capybaras are [herbivores](/diets/herbivore) that graze almost constantly during the morning and evening. **Grasses** make up most of their diet, supplemented with aquatic plants, fruits, reeds, and bark.

Their teeth grow continuously throughout life to handle the wear from chewing tough plant material. Capybaras also practice coprophagy—eating their own droppings—to extract additional nutrients from difficult-to-digest plant matter.

## Social life and the calming capybara

Capybaras live in **herds** of 10 to 20 individuals, led by a dominant male. They communicate through a range of sounds including barks, whistles, and purring. Close bonds form through mutual grooming.

Their famous calm temperament draws other species to them. Birds pick insects from their skin, monkeys lounge on their backs, and even wild caimans have been photographed resting beside them. Read more about [social animals](/topics/social-animals).

## Life cycle and pups

A baby capybara is called a **pup**. After about 150 days of gestation, a female gives birth to a litter of 1 to 8 pups. Pups are precocial—they can walk, swim, and nibble grass within hours of birth.

All females in a herd help care for the young. Pups nurse for several months and stay with the herd as they grow. Males reach maturity at around 18 months and may eventually challenge the dominant male.

## How big and fast are capybaras?

Adult capybaras stand about **20 to 24 inches** (50–60 cm) at the shoulder and can reach over 4 feet (1.3 m) in length. Despite their bulk, they can run at up to **22 mph (35 km/h)** in short bursts.

They are also skilled swimmers. Their **webbed feet**, high-set eyes, and nostrils positioned on top of the snout allow them to see, breathe, and move through water while almost completely submerged.

## Are capybaras at risk?

Capybaras are listed as [Least Concern](/conservation-status/least-concern) with a **stable population** across their wide South American range. They are hunted for meat and hide in some areas, particularly in Venezuela where capybara meat is a traditional food.

Habitat loss from agriculture and development can reduce local populations. In some urban areas, capybaras have adapted to life alongside humans and are protected.
