## What is a Galápagos tortoise?

A Galápagos tortoise is a giant **island reptile** and one of the biggest tortoises in the world. It has a heavy shell, thick scaly skin, and powerful legs that look a bit like those of an elephant.

Its scientific name is *Chelonoidis niger*. Galápagos tortoises are famous for their great size, long life, and calm, slow-moving behavior.

## Where do Galápagos tortoises live?

Galápagos tortoises live only on the **Galápagos Islands** of Ecuador. Different islands have different habitats, from dry lowlands with cactus and shrubs to greener highlands with grasses and cooler air.

Some tortoises have **dome-shaped shells**, while others have **saddleback shells** that rise at the front. These shapes help them use the food available on different islands.

## What do Galápagos tortoises eat?

Galápagos tortoises are [herbivores](/diets/herbivore). They eat **grasses, leaves, flowers, fruit, and cactus pads**.

They can survive dry periods well because they move slowly, lose water slowly, and get moisture from the plants they eat.

## How do Galápagos tortoises live?

These tortoises spend much of the day **grazing, resting, and basking** in the sun. When the weather gets hot, they may wallow in mud or rest in shade to cool down.

They are usually peaceful animals, but they can hiss and pull their head and legs inside the shell if they feel threatened.

## Life cycle and hatchlings

Female tortoises dig nests in dry, sandy soil and lay **eggs** underground. After several months, the babies hatch and dig upward to the surface.

A baby Galápagos tortoise is called a **hatchling**. Hatchlings are tiny compared with the adults and are much more vulnerable to predators.

## Why do they live so long?

Galápagos tortoises have a **slow metabolism**, a tough shell, and a calm lifestyle. Those traits help them use energy slowly and may be one reason they can live for many decades.

They also keep growing for a long time and may not reach full adult size until many years after hatching.

## Why are Galápagos tortoises at risk?

Galápagos tortoises were hunted heavily in the past, and many island populations dropped sharply. Introduced animals such as rats, pigs, and dogs still threaten eggs and hatchlings.

Today, protected areas, nest protection, and captive breeding have helped several tortoise populations recover. Even so, these giants still need careful conservation on the islands.
