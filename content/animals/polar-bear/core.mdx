## What is a polar bear?

A polar bear is the **largest bear** and the biggest land carnivore on Earth. It has creamy white fur, black skin, huge paws, and a thick layer of fat called blubber.

Polar bears belong to the bear family Ursidae. Their scientific name is *Ursus maritimus*, which means "sea bear."

## Where do polar bears live?

Polar bears live in the **Arctic** around Canada, Alaska, Greenland, Norway, and Russia. They travel across **sea ice**, coastal shores, and frozen tundra.

Unlike many bears, polar bears spend much of their lives on ocean ice rather than in forests.

## What do polar bears eat?

Polar bears are [carnivores](/diets/carnivore). They mainly hunt **seals**, especially ringed and bearded seals. A bear may wait quietly at a breathing hole or break into a snow den.

Polar bears can also eat walrus calves, fish, or carcasses when seals are hard to find.

## How do polar bears live?

Adult polar bears usually hunt **alone**, except mothers raising cubs. They are strong swimmers and may cross open water between ice floes.

Mothers dig snow dens where cubs are born in winter and nursed until spring.

## Why are polar bears at risk?

Polar bears are listed as **[Vulnerable](/conservation-status/vulnerable)**. Rising temperatures are melting Arctic sea ice earlier each year, giving bears less time to hunt seals.

Pollution and conflict with people in some Arctic towns also put pressure on local populations.

## Life cycle and cubs

A baby polar bear is called a **cub**. Cubs are born small and blind in a snow den. They grow quickly on rich milk and leave the den in spring.

Cubs stay with their mother for about two and a half years, learning to walk on ice and hunt seals.
