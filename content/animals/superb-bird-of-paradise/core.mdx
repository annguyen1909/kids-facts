## What is a superb bird-of-paradise?

The superb bird-of-paradise (*Lophorina superba*) is a spectacular, world-renowned passerine bird endemic to the misty mountain rainforests of New Guinea. Belonging to the legendary family Paradisaeidae, this avian marvel is globally famous for possessing one of the most hypnotic, complex, and visually stunning courtship displays in the entire animal kingdom.

To the casual observer, a resting male appears to be a sleek, medium-sized black songbird with a subtle green sheen on his head and chest. However, when a female enters his territory, he initiates a breathtaking shape-shifting transformation. Flaring a massive, velvety black cape of feathers forward, he converts his silhouette into a perfect, light-absorbing black disc punctuated by an intensely glowing, neon-green breast shield and matching false eye spots.

## Where does it live?

Superb birds-of-paradise are endemic to the vast island of New Guinea, spanning across both Papua New Guinea and the Indonesian provinces of Papua and West Papua. They thrive in the lush, sloping montane rainforests and high-elevation tropical cloud forests that run along the central mountainous spine of the island, as well as the isolated peaks of the Vogelkop (Bird's Head) Peninsula.

They inhabit steep forest slopes and ridges at elevations typically ranging from 1,000 to 2,300 meters (3,300 to 7,500 feet) above sea level. Within these mist-shrouded cloud forests, the birds navigate the dense middle to upper canopy, utilizing sturdy horizontal branches and exposed fallen tree trunks as the natural stages for their intricate courtship displays.

## What does it eat?

As active forest omnivores, superb birds-of-paradise maintain a rich and varied diet consisting of both invertebrates and plant matter. The protein-rich portion of their diet features a wide array of insects, including crickets, beetles, caterpillars, ants, and spiders, which they actively glean from mossy tree trunks and leaves.

To complement their insect intake, they forage extensively for small, nutrient-rich forest fruits, berries, and seeds found throughout the canopy. They possess a sharp, agile, slightly curved beak that provides excellent precision for snatching fast-moving arthropods out of bark crevices and plucking ripe berries from overhanging vines. Through their fruit-foraging habits, they play a vital ecological role as seed dispersers within the New Guinea rainforest ecosystem.

## How does it behave?

Outside of the breeding season, superb birds-of-paradise are highly solitary, spending their days discreetly foraging among the dense forest branches. Males, however, are fiercely territorial regarding their traditional courtship perches. A mature male will dedicate hours each day to meticulously preparing his dance stage, using his beak to clear away leaves, moss, and loose bark to ensure an unobstructed, pristine presentation space.

The courtship display itself is an extraordinary masterclass in evolutionary optics and behavioral choreography. When an interested female lands on his prepared perch, the male bows forward and instantly erects his massive black cape and iridescent green breast shield. He hops rhythmically in rapid, tight semicircles around the female, keeping his blindingly bright green shield fully oriented toward her eyes while forcefully snapping his wing feathers together to create a rhythmic, acoustic tempo.

## Life cycle and babies

Like many members of the bird-of-paradise family, the species exhibits extreme sexual dimorphism and polygynous breeding habits. Because mature males dedicate virtually their entire lives and energy budgets to defending courtship perches and performing exhausting displays, they take absolutely no part in the nesting process or parental care.

Following successful mating, the female departs the display perch to construct a neat, cup-shaped nest woven from soft plant fibers, moss, and rootlets, hidden discreetly within the dense foliage of a forest tree. She relies entirely on her cryptic, reddish-brown plumage to camouflage herself from predators while incubating a clutch of 1 to 2 eggs for approximately 18 to 20 days. Upon hatching, the altricial chicks are fed a protein-dense diet of insects by the female, growing rapidly and fledging from the nest within three to four weeks.

## Why is it at risk?

The superb bird-of-paradise is currently classified as Least Concern by the IUCN, with wild populations remaining generally stable across their extensive range. Unlike many lowland species, superb birds-of-paradise benefit immensely from their rugged, high-elevation mountain habitat; the steep, inaccessible terrain of New Guinea's cloud forests provides an excellent natural buffer against large-scale commercial logging and agricultural development.

While traditional hunting by indigenous communities for their brilliant display feathers has occurred for centuries, it is largely sustainable and does not represent a severe threat to global population figures. Ongoing environmental monitoring, the establishment of protected forest reserves, and growing eco-tourism initiatives centered around witnessing their magical courtship dance continue to ensure that these spectacular shape-shifters thrive in the wild.
