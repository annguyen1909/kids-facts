## What is a kakapo?

The kakapo (*Strigops habroptilus*) is one of the most remarkable and charismatic avian marvels on Earth. Endemic to New Zealand, this critically endangered night parrot is world-renowned as the only flightless parrot in existence. Often referred to as the "owl parrot" due to its specialized facial disc of feathers and large, forward-facing eyes, the kakapo represents an ancient evolutionary lineage that evolved in isolation over millions of years.

Sporting beautifully speckled moss-green and yellow-brown plumage, the kakapo possesses a remarkably robust, heavy-set body and short, rounded wings. With its charming personality, curious demeanor, and legendary status in wildlife conservation, the kakapo stands as a beloved icon of New Zealand's unique natural heritage.

## Where does it live?

Historically, the kakapo thrived across the entirety of New Zealand's North, South, and Stewart Islands, inhabiting diverse environments ranging from coastal scrublands to dense temperate rainforests. However, following the catastrophic introduction of invasive mammalian predators, wild populations collapsed to the brink of total extinction.

Today, through world-leading conservation science, all surviving kakapo are heavily protected and managed within predator-free offshore island sanctuaries. The primary sanctuaries include Codfish Island (*Whenua Hou*), Anchor Island (*Pukenui*), and Chalky Island (*Te Kakahu-o-Tamatea*). These highly secure islands feature dense native podocarp and beech forests, providing a pristine, predator-free environment where the birds can safely forage and nest on the forest floor.

## What does it eat?

As strict herbivores, kakapo rely entirely on the rich native flora of New Zealand's temperate forests. Their diverse diet includes seeds, fruits, leaves, bark, roots, stems, and mosses. They possess a robust, deeply curved beak perfectly adapted for grinding fibrous plant material and stripping nutritious inner bark from trees.

The absolute cornerstone of the kakapo's ecology is the native rimu tree (*Dacrydium cupressinum*). During the irregular mast seeding years of the rimu, kakapo gorge themselves on the highly nutritious, energy-dense red fruits. This massive influx of calcium and protein is so vital to their physiology that kakapo breeding cycles are completely dependent on the occurrence of these rimu mast events.

## How does it behave?

Kakapo are famously solitary, nocturnal creatures that spend their days roosting quietly under dense ferns or inside hollow tree trunks, emerging at twilight to navigate the dark forest. Because they cannot fly, they have developed massive, muscular legs and powerful claws that make them incredibly adept tree climbers. They can scale towering emergent canopy trees to forage, using their short wings as impromptu parachutes to cushion their descent back to the ground.

To navigate the dark forest floor, kakapo rely on their specialized owl-like facial disc and highly sensitive, whisker-like rictal bristles (*vibrissae*) around their beak to detect obstacles. When startled or sensing potential danger, the kakapo's primary defense mechanism is to freeze completely motionless; their mossy green plumage provides flawless, seamless camouflage amongst the forest foliage.

## Life cycle and babies

The kakapo boasts one of the most unique reproductive systems in the entire avian world: it is the only known parrot to utilize a competitive *lek* breeding system. During a rimu mast year, males gather on prominent ridge tops, digging shallow, bowl-like display hollows in the soil. To attract females, males inflate a massive thoracic air sac and produce deep, resonant, low-frequency booming calls that can echo across the valleys for several kilometers, interspersed with sharp, high-pitched metallic "chinging" sounds.

After mating, females depart the lek to undertake the immense challenge of constructing a nest, incubating the eggs, and rearing the chicks entirely alone. Nesting inside dry cavities at the base of large trees or under rock overhangs, the female lays 1 to 4 white eggs, incubating them for approximately 30 days. The fluffy white chicks hatch defenseless and require dedicated maternal feeding for over three months before fledging. With a highly unhurried lifestyle and low metabolic rate, kakapo are incredibly long-lived, with life expectancies estimated between 60 and 90+ years.

## Why is it at risk?

The kakapo is currently classified as Critically Endangered by the IUCN. Evolving in an ancient island ecosystem completely devoid of land mammals (save for native bats), the kakapo developed its ground-dwelling habits and freezing defense mechanism to hide from avian predators like the extinct Haast's eagle. However, when human settlers arrived and introduced invasive mammalian predators—including stoats, feral cats, ferrets, rats, and dogs—the defenseless, ground-nesting kakapo were swiftly decimated.

By 1995, the global kakapo population had plummeted to a perilous low of just 51 living individuals. In response, the New Zealand Department of Conservation established the groundbreaking Kakapo Recovery Programme. Through intensive round-the-clock monitoring, artificial insemination science, supplementary feeding, and the absolute elimination of invasive predators on sanctuary islands, the kakapo population has achieved a legendary conservation comeback, rising to over 250 living individuals today.
