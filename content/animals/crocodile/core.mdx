## What is a crocodile?

A **crocodile** is a huge reptile with a long snout, tough scales, and one of the strongest bites in the animal kingdom. Nile crocodiles are Africa's most widespread crocodile species.

Nile crocodiles belong to the family Crocodylidae. Their scientific name is *Crocodylus niloticus*.

## Where do crocodiles live?

Nile crocodiles live in **rivers, lakes, and wetlands** across sub-Saharan Africa and Madagascar. They need warm water, sunny banks for basking, and nesting sites near shore.

Famous river crossings in places like the Mara River show how crocodiles gather where prey is abundant.

## What do crocodiles eat?

Crocodiles are [carnivores](/diets/carnivore). They eat fish, birds, mammals, and other animals that come to drink or cross the water. They hunt by **waiting quietly** until prey is close.

Their teeth grip prey, but crocodiles swallow food without chewing it into small pieces first.

## How do crocodiles live?

Crocodiles spend much of the day **basking** on riverbanks to warm up. They are excellent swimmers and can stay hidden with only eyes and nostrils above the surface.

Females build nests and guard eggs until hatchlings emerge.

## Why are crocodiles at risk?

Nile crocodiles are listed as **Least Concern**, but wetland loss and conflict with people can hurt local populations.

Protected rivers and wetlands help crocodiles and many other African animals survive.

## Life cycle and hatchlings

A baby crocodile is called a **hatchling**. Females lay many eggs in nests made of soil or vegetation. After hatching, mothers may carry young to the water in their jaws.

Tiny hatchlings face danger from birds, fish, and other predators.
