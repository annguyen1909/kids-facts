## What is a wandering albatross?

The wandering albatross (*Diomedea exulans*) is a magnificent, legendary icon of the pelagic marine world, universally celebrated as possessing the longest wingspan of any living bird on Earth. Belonging to the specialized order Procellariiformes (the tubenose seabirds), this massive avian represents the pinnacle of aerodynamic evolution, uniquely engineered to conquer the violent, gale-force winds of the Southern Ocean.

Sporting a majestic snowy-white body, spectacular black-tipped wings, and a massive pinkish hooked bill, a mature wandering albatross cuts an unforgettable silhouette against the turbulent sea. For centuries, these majestic gliders have captured the imagination of sailors and explorers, serving as enduring symbols of open-ocean solitude, absolute freedom, and pristine marine wilderness.

## Where does it live?

Wandering albatrosses are true pelagic nomads, inhabiting the vast, circumpolar expanses of the Southern Ocean as well as the southern portions of the Atlantic, Pacific, and Indian Oceans. They spend over 90% of their long lives soaring over open water, completely untethered from land, navigating latitudes from the roaring forties to the screaming sixties.

When they do touch land, it is exclusively to breed on remote, windswept subantarctic islands. Their primary breeding colonies are established on isolated outposts such as South Georgia and the South Sandwich Islands, the Crozet Archipelago, the Kerguelen Islands, the Prince Edward Islands, and Macquarie Island. These rugged, tussock-grass covered islands provide the essential combination of strong prevailing winds for flight takeoff and absolute isolation from terrestrial predators.

## What does it eat?

As specialized pelagic carnivores, wandering albatrosses rely on the rich marine biodiversity of the Southern Ocean. Their diet consists predominantly of cephalopods—specifically bioluminescent squid and cuttlefish—alongside schooling pelagic fish, krill, and marine crustacean swarms. They are also highly opportunistic scavengers, actively feeding on marine mammal carrion and offal discarded by commercial fishing fleets.

They forage primarily by surface seizing, floating buoyantly on the sea surface to snatch unsuspecting squid and fish from the upper water column with their powerful, razor-sharp hooked beaks. Because many of their favorite squid species migrate to the ocean surface at night, wandering albatrosses are highly active nocturnal foragers, utilizing their extraordinary sense of smell and visual acuity to pinpoint bioluminescent prey in pitch-black waters.

## How does it behave?

The defining behavioral hallmark of the wandering albatross is its absolute mastery of dynamic soaring. Instead of relying on exhausting muscular flapping, an albatross continuously extracts energy from the wind shear boundary layers established above ocean waves. By repeatedly banking into the wind to gain elevation and then wheeling downwind to build tremendous speed, they can travel over 1,000 kilometers in a single day virtually effortlessly.

To make this incredible flight mechanism possible, wandering albatrosses possess a specialized anatomical adaptation: a highly specialized tendon lock (*sheet lock*) in their shoulder joints. This mechanism locks their massive wings fully extended at a 180-degree angle, allowing them to glide across oceans for hours or days at a time without expending any muscular energy whatsoever.

## Life cycle and babies

Wandering albatrosses are famously monogamous, forming lifelong partnerships that are reinforced through spectacular, complex courtship displays. Gathering on breeding islands, bonded pairs engage in beautiful synchronized rituals involving ecstatic sky calling, rapid bill snapping, mutual preening, and graceful head bowing.

Because raising a single chick demands such an immense investment of time and energy, successful pairs can only breed every two years. The female lays a single, massive white egg in a raised mound nest made of peat and tussock grass. Both parents share the demanding incubation duties for 78 to 85 days. Upon hatching, the chick is a colossal ball of white down, fed a highly nutritious, energy-dense stomach oil regurgitated by the parents. The chick remains on the nest for nearly 10 months before fledging; once the juvenile finally takes flight, it will spend the next five to ten years flying continuously over the open ocean before ever setting foot on land again to breed.

## Why is it at risk?

The wandering albatross is currently classified as Vulnerable by the IUCN, with wild populations experiencing alarming, long-term declines. The single greatest threat to their survival is accidental bycatch in commercial longline and trawl fishing fisheries operating across the Southern Ocean. When longline vessels deploy thousands of baited hooks into the water, foraging albatrosses frequently attempt to swallow the bait, becoming hooked and dragged beneath the surface to drown.

Additionally, the widespread accumulation of marine plastic pollution poses a severe hazard; adult albatrosses frequently mistake floating plastic debris for squid and feed it to their developing chicks, resulting in fatal starvation and internal obstruction. Fortunately, global conservation initiatives—such as the international Agreement on the Conservation of Albatrosses and Petrels (ACAP)—are actively driving the implementation of vital bycatch mitigation measures, including bird-scaring streamer lines, weighted quick-sinking hooks, and night-setting protocols to safeguard these oceanic wanderers for future generations.
