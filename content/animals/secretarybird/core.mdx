## What is a secretarybird?

The secretarybird (*Sagittarius serpentarius*) is a spectacular, instantly recognizable icon of the African savanna, celebrated as one of the most visually extraordinary birds of prey on Earth. Belonging to its own unique biological family, Sagittariidae, this massive raptor completely breaks the traditional mold; while it possesses the hooked bill and powerful head of an eagle, it stands tall upon long, crane-like legs designed for a highly specialized terrestrial hunting lifestyle.

Sporting an elegant grey-and-black plumage, bare orange facial skin around piercing prominent eyes, and a majestic crest of black quill-like feathers on its head, the secretarybird cuts an unforgettable silhouette across the plains. Renowned for its regal posture and legendary, blindingly fast stomping attacks, it reigns as the undisputed master of terrestrial predation in the African grasslands.

## Where does it live?

Secretarybirds are widely distributed across the open expanses of sub-Saharan Africa. Their extensive range stretches from Senegal in West Africa across to Somalia in the east, and extends down through the rich wildlife corridors of East and Southern Africa, terminating near the Cape of Good Hope in South Africa.

They exclusively inhabit open savannas, grasslands, shrublands, and semi-arid plains, actively avoiding dense forests and true deserts. Within these vast landscapes, secretarybirds require open ground with short to medium grasses for unhindered walking and visibility, interspersed with scattered acacia trees which they rely on for overnight roosting and nesting.

## What does it eat?

As highly active opportunistic carnivores, secretarybirds maintain a diverse diet consisting of both invertebrates and small vertebrates. A significant volume of their daily intake comprises large arthropods, particularly swarming locusts, grasshoppers, beetles, and scorpions. However, their legendary reputation stems from their mastery in hunting tough vertebrate prey, including monitor lizards, tortoises, small rodents, young birds, and famously, venomous snakes like cobras and puff adders.

To secure a meal, a secretarybird actively strides across the grass, constantly scanning the terrain with its acute binocular vision. When prey is spotted, the bird initiates an extraordinary, blindingly fast stomping attack. Flaring its wings for balance and distraction, it snaps its powerful feet downward with a force exceeding five times its own bodyweight, making contact in just 15 milliseconds—instantaneously breaking the spine or crushing the skull of its catch.

## How does it behave?

The defining behavioral hallmark of the secretarybird is its daily terrestrial striding. Unlike other raptors that hunt from the sky or prominent perches, a secretarybird spends almost its entire day walking across the earth, frequently covering 20 to 30 kilometers (12 to 18 miles) in a single day. Despite their ground-dwelling habits, they are exceptionally powerful fliers; when traversing vast territories or engaging in courtship displays, they soar effortlessly on thermal currents with their long legs trailing gracefully behind them.

To survive the hazards of hunting venomous reptiles, secretarybirds possess superb anatomical adaptations. Their lower legs are protected by thick, heavily modified scales that act as impenetrable armor plates against snake fangs. Additionally, they use their large wings as artificial shields, intercepting the desperate strikes of cornered snakes before delivering the fatal stomp.

## Life cycle and babies

Secretarybirds are strictly monogamous, forming lifelong partnerships that are reinforced through mutual preening, synchronized terrestrial walks, and spectacular aerial courtship flights where pairs chase each other and perform breathtaking talons-locked downward spirals.

Unlike almost all other ground-dwelling birds, secretarybirds construct massive stick nests high up in the thorny crowns of flat-topped acacia trees, perfectly insulating their eggs and chicks from terrestrial predators. The female lays 1 to 3 chalky white eggs, which both parents diligently incubate for 42 to 46 days. Upon hatching, the altricial chicks are covered in white down and are fed regurgitated insects and small reptiles by both parents. The chicks develop slowly on the treetop platform, exercising their long legs for many weeks before taking their first ambitious flights around three months of age.

## Why is it at risk?

The secretarybird is currently classified as Endangered by the IUCN, with remaining wild populations experiencing severe, rapid declines across much of their historical range. The single greatest threat to their survival is widespread, accelerating habitat loss and degradation caused by the conversion of pristine African savannas into commercial farmland, severe livestock overgrazing, and urban expansion.

Because secretarybirds require massive, undisturbed terrestrial home ranges to maintain their active walking lifestyle, habitat fragmentation directly limits their ability to locate sufficient prey resources. Furthermore, direct collisions with newly erected high-voltage power lines and livestock fences, along with secondary poisoning from agricultural insecticides deployed to kill locust swarms, exert immense downward pressure on their numbers. Strict habitat preservation, eco-friendly infrastructure design, and community conservation awareness are critical to ensuring this marching eagle continues to grace the African plains.
