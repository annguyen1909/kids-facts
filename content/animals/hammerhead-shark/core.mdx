## What is a great hammerhead shark?

The great hammerhead shark is a large, powerful marine predator famous for its unique, hammer-shaped head. Known scientifically as *Sphyrna mokarran*, it is the largest of all nine species of hammerhead sharks.

Their distinctive hammer head is called a **cephalofoil**. Under normal swimming, this wide, flat head acts like a wing, providing lift and helping the shark make sharp, rapid turns in the water. The shark's eyes are located on the far outer edges of the "hammer," giving them outstanding 360-degree vision. 

As cartilaginous fish (Class Chondrichthyes), great hammerheads have skeletons made of flexible cartilage rather than bone, have skin covered in tiny, tooth-like scales called dermal denticles, and breathe through gill slits.

## Where do great hammerhead sharks live?

Great hammerhead sharks are found in warm temperate and tropical waters worldwide. 

They inhabit coastal areas and open oceans in the **Atlantic, Pacific, and Indian Oceans**, as well as the Mediterranean and Caribbean Seas. They can be found in shallow waters near shore—such as bays and estuaries—but they also travel to deep waters over 260 feet (80 meters) down. They love hanging out around **coral reefs, coral walls, and continental shelves** where marine life is abundant. In summer, they migrate toward cooler waters closer to the poles.

## What do great hammerhead sharks eat?

Great hammerhead sharks are top **carnivores** that hunt a variety of marine life. They are highly efficient hunters that use their unique bodies to catch fast-moving prey.

Their absolute favorite food is **stingrays**. They also eat groupers, jacks, herrings, squid, octopuses, crabs, and smaller sharks (including other hammerheads). They are famous for using their wide cephalofoils to pin stingrays flat against the sandy ocean floor, preventing the ray from escaping while the shark bites.

## How do great hammerhead sharks behave?

Unlike many other species of hammerheads that swim in large schools, the great hammerhead is a **solitary predator** that spends most of its time traveling and hunting alone. They are active during both the day and night.

They swim with a unique side-to-side sweeping motion of their heads, which allows them to scan a large area of the sea floor. Their wide heads are packed with thousands of microscopic sensory pores called **Ampullae of Lorenzini**. These pores can detect the tiny electrical fields produced by the heartbeats and muscle movements of prey, allowing the hammerhead to find stingrays completely buried under the sand!

## Life cycle and baby great hammerhead sharks

Unlike many sharks that lay eggs in the water, great hammerheads are viviparous, meaning they give birth to **live babies**. 

A female hammerhead carries her developing pups inside her body for about 11 months. During the spring or summer, she swims into shallow, protected coastal bays or estuaries to give birth. A single female can give birth to a large litter of **6 to 42 babies**, which are called **pups**.

At birth, the pups are about 2 feet (60 cm) long. Their hammer-shaped heads are soft and flexible during birth so they don't harm the mother, but they harden quickly after birth. The pups are fully independent immediately, swimming away to find food and hide in shallow mangroves to stay safe from larger sharks.

## Why are great hammerhead sharks at risk?

The great hammerhead shark is currently listed as **Critically Endangered** by the IUCN, meaning they face an extremely high risk of extinction in the wild.

The greatest threat to hammerheads is **overfishing**. They are highly prized for the global shark fin trade due to their large dorsal fins, and are often caught illegally (a practice called finning). They are also frequently caught accidentally as bycatch in commercial fishing nets and longlines meant for other fish. Because they are highly sensitive to stress, hammerheads often die quickly once caught in nets, even if they are released.

Protecting coral reefs, establishing marine protected areas, and banning shark finning are critical steps needed to save these unique ocean predators.
