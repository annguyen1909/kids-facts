## What is a penguin?

A penguin is a **flightless seabird** built for life in cold ocean water. It has a stout body, flipper-shaped wings, short legs, and waterproof feathers that trap a layer of warm air.

Penguins belong to the bird family *Spheniscidae*. Their stiff wings work like paddles, letting them "fly" underwater while chasing fish and krill.

## Where does it live?

Almost all penguins live in the **Southern Hemisphere**, from icy Antarctica to rocky coasts in South America, Africa, Australia, and nearby islands. Emperor and Adélie penguins breed on Antarctic ice, while species such as the African penguin nest on southern beaches.

Penguins need cold, nutrient-rich seas near land or ice where they can nest and dive for food. Only the Galápagos penguin lives near the equator, north of most other species.

## What does it eat?

Penguins are [carnivores](/diets/carnivore). They eat fish, krill, squid, and other small ocean animals.

A penguin catches prey by **diving** and swimming underwater. Some species dive hundreds of feet deep and hold their breath for many minutes while searching for food.

## How does it behave?

Most penguin species nest in large, noisy **colonies** where parents share duties. One adult may guard the egg or chick while the other swims out to feed. Explore more [social animals](/topics/social-animals).

On land, penguins waddle or slide on their bellies across ice. In the water, they swim in groups called rafts and may leap onto ice in a splashy move called porpoising.

## Life cycle and babies

A baby penguin is called a **chick**. Parents usually lay one or two eggs and take turns keeping them warm until they hatch.

Chicks are covered in fluffy down at first. They stay near the nest or in a crèche group while learning to swim, dive, and find food on their own.

## How big and fast is it?

Penguin size varies by species. The little penguin stands barely a foot tall, while the emperor penguin can reach about **four feet** and weigh nearly 90 pounds.

In the water, penguins are fast and agile. Many swim several miles per hour, and some species can burst up to about **22 mph** when chasing prey or escaping a predator.

## Why is it at risk?

Many penguin populations are under pressure. Climate change, overfishing, oil spills, and habitat disturbance make it harder for penguins to find food and raise chicks safely.

Several species are listed as threatened or [Vulnerable](/conservation-status/vulnerable). Protecting ocean food webs and coastal nesting sites helps penguins survive for future generations.
