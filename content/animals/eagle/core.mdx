## What is an eagle?

An eagle is a **large bird of prey** with powerful wings, sharp eyes, and strong talons. The bald eagle—famous as the national bird of the United States—has a brown body, white head, and yellow hooked beak.

Eagles belong to the hawk and eagle family *Accipitridae*. The bald eagle's scientific name is *Haliaeetus leucocephalus*.

## Where do eagles live?

Bald eagles live across **North America**, from Alaska and Canada to the lower United States and parts of Mexico. They stay near **lakes, rivers, coasts, and wetlands** where fish and water birds are easy to find.

Tall trees or cliffs give eagles safe places to nest and watch for prey. In winter, some eagles move to open water that stays unfrozen.

## What do eagles eat?

Eagles are [carnivores](/diets/carnivore). They eat fish, ducks, gulls, small mammals, and carrion (animals that have already died).

Bald eagles are skilled fish hunters. They may swoop low over the water, snatch a fish with their talons, and carry it to a perch to eat.

## How do eagles hunt and live?

Eagles usually live in **mated pairs**, not big groups. A pair may defend a territory around their nest and return to the same area year after year.

They hunt by **soaring high** and watching with excellent vision, then diving or gliding down to grab prey. Strong legs and sharp talons lock onto fish and birds in mid-grab.

## Life cycle and eaglets

A baby eagle is called an **eaglet**. Parents lay one to three eggs and take turns keeping them warm for about five weeks until they hatch.

Young eagles are covered in gray down at first. They stay in the nest for months while parents bring food, then learn to fly and hunt on their own.

## How big and fast are eagles?

Adult bald eagles are among the largest birds of prey in North America. Their **wingspan** can reach about 6 to 7.5 feet, and females are often larger than males.

Eagles soar on rising air currents to save energy. When diving after prey, a bald eagle can reach speeds near **100 mph (160 km/h)**.

## Why are eagles at risk?

Adult eagles have few natural enemies, but they still face threats from **habitat loss, pollution, and fishing gear**. In the 1900s, bald eagle numbers dropped sharply in the United States.

Laws protecting eagles and banning harmful pesticides helped populations recover. Today, bald eagles are a conservation success story across much of North America.
