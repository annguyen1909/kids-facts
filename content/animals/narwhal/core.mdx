## What is a narwhal?

A narwhal is a medium-sized whale that lives in the icy waters of the Arctic. It is famous for its long spiral **tusk**, which is actually a tooth that grows through the whale's upper lip. Narwhals belong to the same family as beluga whales—[Monodontidae](/families/monodontidae).

Their scientific name, *Monodon monoceros*, means "one tooth, one horn" in Greek.

## Where do narwhals live?

Narwhals live in the **Arctic Ocean** and surrounding seas near Canada, Greenland, Norway, and Russia. They spend winters deep under pack ice and migrate to coastal waters in summer.

The extreme cold and frozen landscape suits them perfectly. Their flexible spine and lack of a dorsal fin allow them to navigate tight channels under sea ice. Explore more [Arctic animals](/habitats/arctic).

## What do narwhals eat?

Narwhals are [carnivores](/diets/carnivore) that hunt fish and invertebrates. Their main prey includes **Arctic cod**, Greenland halibut, squid, and shrimp.

They are remarkable divers—capable of plunging more than 5,000 feet (1,500 m) and holding their breath for up to 25 minutes. Narwhals use **echolocation** to find prey in dark, deep water beneath the ice.

## The narwhal's extraordinary tusk

The narwhal's tusk is its most recognizable feature. It is actually the **upper left canine tooth** of male narwhals, spiraling counter-clockwise as it grows up to 10 feet (3 m) long.

Scientists believe the tusk contains up to **10 million nerve endings** that sense changes in water temperature, pressure, and salinity. Males sometimes fence with their tusks in a behavior called "tusking," which may be used to establish dominance. Read more about [unusual animal adaptations](/topics/adaptations).

## Life cycle and calves

A baby narwhal is called a **calf**. After a gestation of about 14 months, a female gives birth to a single calf in summer. Calves are dark grey at birth and lighten as they age.

Narwhals live in pods and are social animals. Mothers nurse their calves for up to 20 months. Young narwhals gradually darken again as adults before lightening once more in old age.

## How big are narwhals?

Adult narwhals measure 13 to 20 feet (4–6 m) in body length and can weigh up to 3,500 pounds (1,600 kg). A **thick blubber layer** insulates them against the freezing Arctic water and provides energy reserves during lean seasons.

Males are typically larger than females and almost always carry the long tusk. Some rare individuals grow two tusks.

## Why are narwhals at risk?

Narwhals are listed as [Near Threatened](/conservation-status/near-threatened). **Climate change** is the biggest danger—melting sea ice disrupts the habitat they depend on and affects the fish populations they eat.

Narwhals are also hunted by some Indigenous Arctic communities for food and materials. Shipping traffic and oil exploration in Arctic waters add further pressure on this unique whale.
