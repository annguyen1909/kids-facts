## What is a hippopotamus?

A hippopotamus—often called a **hippo**—is one of the **largest land mammals** on Earth. It has a barrel-shaped body, short legs, and a huge mouth with long canine teeth.

Hippos are semiaquatic mammals native to Africa. Their scientific name is *Hippopotamus amphibius*.

## Where do hippos live?

Wild hippos live in **rivers, lakes, and wetlands** across sub-Saharan Africa. They spend most daylight hours resting in water, then move onto land after dark to feed.

Healthy rivers with deep pools and nearby grasslands are essential hippo habitat. Explore more [savanna animals](/habitats/savanna) that share these landscapes with hippos.

## What do hippos eat?

Hippos are **herbivores**. They mainly eat **grass**, plus some reeds and other plants they find on land.

Despite their size and big teeth, hippos do not hunt other animals for food. They can travel several miles in a night while grazing.

## How do hippos live?

Hippos gather in groups called **pods**, often with females and young sharing the same stretch of river. Adult males defend territories and may fight with loud splashing, yawning displays, and charges.

Hippos communicate with grunts, honks, and bellows—some calls even carry underwater.

## Why are hippos at risk?

Hippos are listed as **[Vulnerable](/conservation-status/vulnerable)**. Their numbers have dropped in many regions because of habitat loss, drought, and illegal hunting for meat and ivory-like teeth.

Protected wetlands and river parks give hippos safer places to live and raise calves.

## Life cycle and calves

A baby hippo is called a **calf**. Calves are born in water and must reach the surface quickly to breathe. They nurse underwater and stay very close to their mothers for months.

Young hippos learn to follow the pod, graze on grass, and avoid territorial bulls as they grow.
