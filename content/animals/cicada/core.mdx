# Cicada

## The Roaring Chorus of the [Forest](/habitats/forest)
The periodical cicada (*Magicicada septendecim*) stands as one of nature’s most unique biological phenomena. Famous for a staggering 17-year subterranean life cycle, these insects spend over a decade and a half living quietly out of sight before emerging in billions to dominate the temperate forests of North America. When they finally take to the trees, the males unleash a deafening, mechanical buzz that can reach up to 100 decibels—equivalent to the roar of a chainsaw. Weighing mere grams but emerging in numbers so vast they visibly alter the forest landscape, the periodical cicada's brief, frenzied adult life is a masterclass in survival through sheer volume.

## Taxonomy and Evolution
Scientifically classified as *Magicicada septendecim*, the 17-year periodical cicada falls within the kingdom Animalia, the phylum Arthropoda, and the class Insecta. As a member of the order Hemiptera (the "true bugs"), it is closely related to aphids and leafhoppers, and is placed in the family [Cicadidae](/families/cicadidae). 

Evolutionary entomologists theorize that the cicada's extreme, prime-numbered life cycle (13 or 17 years) evolved during the Pleistocene epoch as a mechanism to avoid climatic fluctuations and strictly outpace the life cycles of potential predators. By synchronizing their emergence precisely every 17 years, *Magicicada* prevents any specialized predator population from matching their exact schedule.

## Anatomy and Physical Characteristics
The adult periodical cicada is a striking creature. Measuring between 2.4 and 3.3 cm in length, they are characterized by stark black bodies, brilliant ruby-red eyes set wide apart on their heads, and delicate, membranous wings laced with stark orange veins. 

Like all true bugs, they possess highly specialized mouthparts. Rather than chewing mandibles, they possess a rigid, needle-like structure called a rostrum. This anatomical feature functions as a hypodermic straw, allowing the cicada to pierce the dense bark of twigs to access the vascular tissue beneath. 

The most defining anatomical feature, exclusive to the males, is the tymbal organ. Located on the abdomen, the tymbal consists of a series of ribbed, drum-like membranes. Using powerful muscles, the male buckles and unbuckles these ribs hundreds of times per second. The resulting sound is amplified by the insect's largely hollow abdomen, creating the deafening drone that defines a cicada emergence.

## Habitat and Distribution
Periodical cicadas are exclusively endemic to the eastern and central United States, heavily distributed across deciduous and temperate forests. Because their survival strategy relies entirely on overwhelming numbers, they are deeply tethered to environments with a high density of mature, deciduous trees such as oak, maple, and hickory. 

Their habitat is effectively split into two distinct biomes across their lifespan: the subterranean soil biome, where nymphs reside up to two feet beneath the surface attached to tree roots, and the arboreal canopy, which they briefly occupy as winged adults to mate and lay eggs.

## Diet and Feeding Strategies
Cicadas are strict herbivores, feeding entirely on the xylem fluid (sap) of trees and woody plants. 

Their feeding strategy changes drastically between their developmental stages. For the 17 years spent underground as a nymph, the cicada utilizes powerful, digging front claws to excavate small chambers around tree roots. It inserts its rostrum into the root to continuously siphon fluid, growing agonizingly slowly due to the incredibly low nutritional value of root xylem. 

When they emerge as adults, their feeding is minimal and secondary to reproduction. However, they still utilize their rostrum to pierce the tender branches and twigs of canopy trees to drink sap, sustaining themselves just long enough to complete their mating rituals before dying a few weeks later. 

## Social Structure and Behavior
The defining behavioral strategy of the periodical cicada is "predator satiation." They do not possess stingers, venom, or evasive speed. Instead, they rely on mass emergence. Millions, sometimes billions, of cicadas erupt from the soil within the exact same week. Predators—birds, squirrels, wasps, spiders, and domestic pets—gorge themselves until they are physically incapable of eating more. By providing an infinite all-you-can-eat buffet, a massive percentage of the cicada population guarantees their own survival.

The adult phase is entirely consumed by a chaotic mating frenzy. The deafening sound of the forest is driven by male choruses attempting to attract females. Females respond to preferred males with a quiet, clicking flick of their wings. 

## Life Cycle and Reproduction
The reproductive cycle of the 17-year cicada is an exercise in extreme patience and sudden urgency. After mating, the female uses a specialized appendage called an ovipositor to slice tiny, V-shaped slits into living tree branches. She deposits roughly 400 to 600 eggs across several branches. 

After an incubation period of 6 to 10 weeks, the eggs hatch into tiny, ant-sized nymphs. These neonate nymphs immediately free-fall from the canopy to the forest floor and burrow into the soil. Once underground, they attach themselves to a root and remain there for 17 years. When their internal biological clocks signal the 17th spring, and soil temperatures reach exactly 64°F (18°C), the nymphs burrow to the surface en masse. They climb the nearest vertical surface—a tree trunk, fence, or wall—and undergo their final molt. They split their brown, crusty exoskeletons down the back, emerging as soft, pale adults that must wait hours for their new wings to harden before taking flight.

## Conservation Status and Threats

*(Population and conservation trend data sourced from the IUCN Red List of Threatened Species)*

Currently, *Magicicada septendecim* is classified as a species of "Least Concern." The sheer scale of their emergences makes them seemingly invulnerable. 

However, they are highly susceptible to long-term environmental changes. Widespread deforestation and urban development effectively pave over their subterranean habitats, entombing sleeping nymphs under concrete. Because a brood relies on massive, undisturbed root systems for 17 years, clear-cutting a forest guarantees the total eradication of that specific local population. Additionally, climate change and unseasonal warming trends have begun to trigger "straggler" emergences, causing small fractions of a brood to emerge years early, immediately falling victim to predators because they lack the protection of numbers.
