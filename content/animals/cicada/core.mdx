## What is a cicada?

A cicada is a medium-to-large insect famous all over the world for its loud, buzzing summer songs and unique life cycle. Known scientifically as *Magicicada septendecim* (for the 17-year periodical species), they belong to the order of true bugs, Hemiptera.

Cicadas have stout bodies, broad heads, and large, prominent compound eyes that are usually bright red in periodical species and black or green in annual species. They have four clear, membranous wings with thick, dark veins that they fold tent-like over their backs when resting. Despite their large size, they do not bite or sting and are completely harmless to humans.

As insects, cicadas have six legs, an exoskeleton, three body parts, and lay eggs.

## Where do cicadas live?

Cicadas are found on every continent except Antarctica. However, the famous **periodical cicadas** (which emerge only every 13 or 17 years) are native only to the eastern and central regions of the **United States**.

They are highly arboreal insects, meaning they live in trees. They make their homes in **temperate deciduous forests, woodlands, parks, orchards, and gardens**. Periodical cicadas spend almost their entire lives underground near tree roots, only climbing up tree trunks into the canopy when they transform into flying adults.

## What do cicadas eat?

Cicadas are **herbivores** that drink plant fluids. They do not have chewing mouthparts; instead, they have a needle-like straw mouth called a rostrum.

As **nymphs** living underground, they poke their straw-like mouths into plant roots to drink xylem fluid, a watery sap containing minerals. 

As **adults**, they use their rostrums to pierce twigs and young branches of hardwood trees (like oak, maple, and ash) to drink sweet tree sap. Because they drink watery fluids, they feed constantly to get enough nutrients.

## How do cicadas behave?

The most famous behavior of the cicada is the **loud buzzing song** made by male cicadas during the summer. Males sing to attract female mates and to establish territory. They make this sound using special ribbed, drum-like organs on the sides of their abdomens called **tymbals**. By contracting their muscles rapidly, the tymbals buckle and vibrate, producing a clicking sound that is amplified by their hollow abdomens. A large chorus of cicadas can produce sounds louder than 90 decibels!

Another unique behavior is their **synchronized emergence**. Periodical cicadas emerge from the soil in billions all at once. By swarming in such massive numbers, they overwhelm predators like birds and squirrels. The predators eat as many as they can, but there are so many cicadas that the vast majority survive to mate and lay eggs.

## Life cycle and baby cicadas

The life cycle of the periodical cicada is one of the wonders of the animal kingdom. A female cicada uses a sharp ovipositor to cut slits in tree twigs and lays a clutch of **400 to 600 small eggs**.

After 6 to 10 weeks, the tiny baby cicadas (called **nymphs**) hatch. They look like tiny white termites. They drop from the branches to the ground and immediately burrow into the soil, digging down up to 2 feet deep. 

The nymphs spend the next **17 years underground** in total darkness, feeding on root sap and growing slowly, shedding their skin five times. 

In the spring of the 17th year, when the soil temperature warms up, they dig tunnels back to the surface. They crawl out of the ground, climb up tree trunks or fences, and shed their skins one last time to reveal their wings, leaving their empty, crunchy brown skins behind.

## Why are cicadas at risk?

Globally, cicadas are listed as a species of **Least Concern** because their numbers are massive. However, they face environmental challenges.

The biggest threat to periodical cicadas is **habitat destruction**. When forests are cleared for housing, shopping centers, or agriculture, the trees they depend on for food and nesting are destroyed. If trees are removed during their 17-year underground phase, the nymphs starve and die.

Pesticides and lawn chemicals can also kill cicadas as they emerge from the ground. Climate change is another concern, as changing temperatures can disrupt the timing of their emergence. Protecting mature forests ensures these ancient, buzzing insects will continue to fill our summer nights with song.
