## What is a ruby-throated hummingbird?

The ruby-throated hummingbird is a tiny, glittering bird belonging to the hummingbird family Trochilidae. It is famous for its **metallic green back and crown**, white chest, and the male's **shimmering ruby-red throat** (called a gorget) which glows like a jewel in the sunlight. Females have a plain white throat.

Their scientific name is *Archilochus colubris*. Among birds, they are some of the smallest creatures on the planet, yet they possess extraordinary flying skills that set them apart from all other birds.

## Where do ruby-throated hummingbirds live?

Ruby-throated hummingbirds breed across **eastern North America**, from southern Canada down to the Gulf Coast of the United States. They inhabit **deciduous and pine forests, orchard edges, gardens, parks, and backyard meadows** where flowers and feeders are present.

During winter, they migrate south to warm areas in **Mexico, Central America, and the Caribbean**. They are highly adaptable birds, choosing places filled with nectar-producing flowers and insects.

## What do hummingbirds eat?

To keep up their high energy, ruby-throated hummingbirds are **omnivores** that feed constantly. They eat:
- **Flower Nectar**: They drink sugary nectar from bright tube-shaped flowers, especially red ones like honeysuckle, petunias, and bee balm.
- **Insects and Spiders**: They catch tiny insects (like gnats, fruit flies, and aphids) in mid-air or pluck them from spider webs. Insects provide essential protein and fat.
- **Tree Sap**: They will drink sweet sap flowing from holes drilled by woodpeckers when flowers are scarce.

## How do hummingbirds behave?

Hummingbirds are aggressive, solitary, and **highly territorial** birds. They will fiercely guard their favorite flower patches and feeders from other hummingbirds, chasing them away with loud chirps and fast dives.

They are also the acrobat experts of the bird world! They have unique shoulder joints that allow their wings to rotate in a figure-eight pattern. This movement lets them **fly backwards, upside down, and hover in place** like a helicopter. They flap their wings up to 80 times per second, creating a soft buzzing hum.

## Life cycle and baby hummingbirds

A hummingbird's nest is a masterpiece of engineering. The female builds a cup about the **size of a ping-pong ball** on a thin tree branch. She uses plant down and lichens for structure and binds it all together with **sticky spider webs** that allow the nest to stretch as the babies grow.

She lays two tiny white eggs, about the size of jellybeans. The mother feeds her chicks a mixture of regurgitated insects and nectar until they are ready to fly on their own after about three weeks.

## Why are hummingbirds at risk?

Ruby-throated hummingbirds are currently listed as Least Concern, but they still face threats from **habitat loss** as forests are cleared, and **collisions with glass windows** during migration.

Climate change also affects their migration schedules and when flowers bloom. To help, people plant native tube-shaped flowers and hang clean sugar-water feeders in their gardens to provide reliable food stations.
