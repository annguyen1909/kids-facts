## What is a praying mantis?

A praying mantis is a fascinating predatory insect belonging to the order Mantodea. They are named for their **unique front legs**, which are bent and held together in a posture that looks like they are praying. 

Mantises have a triangular head, very large compound eyes, and a highly flexible neck. In fact, they are the only insects that can turn their heads **a full 180 degrees** to look over their shoulders!

Their scientific name is *Mantis religiosa*. As insects, they have six legs, three body parts (head, thorax, and abdomen), and a tough outer shell called an exoskeleton.

## Where do praying mantises live?

Praying mantises are found in warm and temperate regions all over the world, including Europe, Asia, Africa, and North America. They prefer habitats with plenty of vegetation, such as **meadows, grasslands, forests, fields, and home gardens**.

They rely on plants, tall grass, and flowering shrubs to hide. Their green or brown bodies act as **perfect camouflage**, making them look exactly like leaves or twigs. This allows them to stay hidden from hungry birds while waiting to surprise their prey.

## What do praying mantises eat?

Praying mantises are strict [carnivores](/diets/carnivore) and excellent hunters. They are **ambush predators**, which means they sit completely still, blending in with plants, until an insect walks or flies close by. 

Then, in the blink of an eye, the mantis strikes! They grab their prey using their powerful front legs, which are lined with **sharp spikes** to keep the prey from escaping. 

A mantis's diet consists mainly of other insects like flies, crickets, grasshoppers, moths, caterpillars, and bees. Very large mantises have even been known to catch small frogs, lizards, and tiny birds!

## How do praying mantises behave?

Praying mantises are solitary creatures, which means they **live and hunt alone**. They are highly territorial and do not share their space with other mantises. 

To defend themselves from predators like bats, mantises have a single ear on the underside of their belly that can detect ultrasonic sounds. If a bat is near, the mantis will tuck its legs and dive out of the air to escape. 

When threatened by a larger predator on the ground, a mantis will stand tall, spread its front legs wide, and hiss to make itself look bigger and scarier.

## Life cycle and baby mantises

The life of a praying mantis has three stages: egg, nymph, and adult. In autumn, the female mantis lays a cluster of eggs in a frothy liquid. This foam quickly hardens into a protective, paper-like case called an **ootheca**. The eggs sleep inside the ootheca through the cold winter.

In spring, the eggs hatch into baby mantises called **nymphs**. Nymphs look like tiny versions of their parents but do not have wings. As they grow, they shed their hard outer skin (molting) several times. Eventually, they grow wings and become fully mature adults.

## Why are praying mantises at risk?

European mantises are listed as Least Concern, meaning their populations are healthy and stable. They are highly beneficial to humans because they eat many insects that can damage farm crops and garden flowers.

However, they still face threats from **habitat loss** as wild fields and meadows are cleared for buildings, and the use of chemical pesticides in gardens, which can kill both the mantises and the insects they rely on for food. Many gardeners welcome mantises as natural protectors for their plants.
