## What is a ladybug?

A ladybug is a small, colorful beetle belonging to the family Coccinellidae. They are best known for their **bright red or orange shells** covered in black spots. 

Although we call them ladybugs or ladybirds, they are not actually bugs or birds—they are **beetles**! They have a dome-shaped body, short antennae, and six black legs. 

Their scientific name is *Coccinella septempunctata*, which refers to the seven spots on their back. Underneath their hard outer shell (called elytra), they have a pair of delicate wings that fold out when they fly.

## Where do ladybugs live?

Ladybugs are highly adaptable and live in temperate climates across Europe, Asia, and North America. They prefer habitats with plenty of plants and flowers, such as **meadows, prairies, deciduous forests, agricultural fields, and suburban gardens**.

They choose locations where their favorite food is plentiful. They spend most of their time crawling along green leaves, flower buds, and stems in search of tiny insect prey.

## What do ladybugs eat?

Ladybugs are hungry predators and strict [carnivores](/diets/carnivore). Their absolute favorite food is the **aphid**, a tiny, soft-bodied insect that sucks sap from plants and damages crops. 

A single ladybug can eat up to **50 to 100 aphids per day**! Because they consume so many garden pests, gardeners and farmers love ladybugs and welcome them as natural pest control.

Both adult ladybugs and their growing larvae hunt constantly. They also eat other soft-bodied pests, such as spider mites, scale insects, thrips, and insect eggs.

## How do ladybugs behave?

Ladybugs are solitary insects that live and hunt alone during the spring and summer. However, they have a very interesting way of defending themselves. 

When a bird or spider tries to eat a ladybug, it will play dead. It will also ooze a tiny bit of yellow, smelly liquid from its leg joints. This is called **reflex bleeding**, and the liquid tastes extremely bitter, which teaches predators to leave them alone.

During the cold autumn months, ladybugs migrate to dry, sheltered spots like under logs, tree bark, or inside houses. They gather in **massive clusters** of thousands of beetles to keep warm and sleep through the winter (hibernation).

## Life cycle and baby ladybugs

A ladybug's life begins as a tiny, yellow egg. Female ladybugs lay egg clusters on the undersides of leaves, always placing them near aphid colonies so the babies have food immediately.

After a few days, the egg hatches into a **larva**. Ladybug larvae look like tiny black alligators with orange markings and spikes. They eat ravenously and grow quickly, shedding their skin several times. 

Eventually, the larva attaches to a leaf and forms a hard case called a **pupa**. Inside, the body transforms, and after about a week, a fully grown adult ladybug emerges.

## Why are ladybugs at risk?

Seven-spotted ladybugs are very common and currently listed as Least Concern. They are highly beneficial to agriculture, protecting millions of plants from insect damage.

However, they are threatened by **pesticide use** in gardens and farms, which kills ladybugs directly and removes their food supply. They also face competition from non-native ladybug species introduced by humans. To help them, people are encouraged to avoid chemical sprays and plant native flowers.
