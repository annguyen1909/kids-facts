## What is a seahorse?

A **seahorse** is a tiny fish with a horse-like head, a tube-shaped snout, and a tail that can curl around plants. Lined seahorses swim upright and are masters of camouflage.

Lined seahorses belong to the family Syngnathidae. Their scientific name is *Hippocampus erectus*.

## Where do seahorses live?

Lined seahorses live in **coastal Atlantic waters** from Canada to Brazil. They are often found in **seagrass beds**, estuaries, and sheltered reef areas.

Their tails grip plants so they are not swept away by currents.

## What do seahorses eat?

Seahorses are [carnivores](/diets/carnivore). They eat tiny shrimp, plankton, and other small animals. They have no teeth and **suck prey** through their long snout.

Because they lack a stomach, seahorses must eat often throughout the day.

## How do seahorses live?

Seahorses usually move slowly and rely on hiding rather than fast swimming. Pairs may stay together for a breeding season.

In one of nature's rarest roles, **male seahorses carry the eggs** in a brood pouch until the fry hatch.

## Why are seahorses at risk?

Lined seahorses are listed as **Vulnerable**. Seagrass loss, pollution, and trapping for aquariums threaten wild populations.

Protecting coastal habitats helps seahorses, sea turtles, and many other ocean animals.

## Life cycle and fry

A baby seahorse is called a **fry**. The female deposits eggs into the male's pouch. After weeks of care, hundreds of tiny fry may swim out into the water.

Fry must find food and shelter quickly in the open ocean or coastal plants.
