## What is a cassowary?

A cassowary is a large **flightless bird** native to the rainforests of Australia and New Guinea. It is widely regarded as the world's most dangerous bird, armed with a dagger-like inner toe claw and powerful legs capable of delivering a devastating kick.

The southern cassowary (*Casuarius casuarius*) is the largest of three cassowary species. It belongs to the family [Casuariidae](/families/casuariidae) and is distantly related to ostriches and emus. Explore more [rainforest animals](/habitats/tropical-rainforest).

## Where do cassowaries live?

Cassowaries live in the **dense tropical rainforests** of northeastern Australia (mainly Queensland), Papua New Guinea, and parts of Indonesia. They are solitary and territorial, each individual patrolling a large area of forest.

They depend on undisturbed rainforest with abundant fruiting trees. Fragmented or degraded habitat makes it harder for cassowaries to find enough food and a mate during breeding season. Learn about [threatened habitats](/topics/habitat-loss).

## What do cassowaries eat?

Cassowaries are [omnivores](/diets/omnivore) that eat mainly **fallen fruit**. They are one of the few animals able to safely digest the fruits of many rainforest plants that are toxic to other species.

They also eat fungi, insects, worms, small vertebrates, and carrion. As they move through the forest they deposit seeds in their droppings, making them vital **seed dispersers** for more than 150 plant species. Without cassowaries, some rainforest plants may not be able to reproduce at all.

## The casque, claws, and danger

The cassowary's most iconic features are its **bony casque** and its inner toe claw. The casque is a hollow, helmet-like growth that sits on top of the head. Scientists debate its purpose—possible functions include pushing aside vegetation, amplifying low-frequency calls, and showing dominance.

The inner toe carries a straight claw up to **4 inches (10 cm)** long. When threatened, a cassowary kicks forward and downward with tremendous force. This claw can inflict deep lacerations. Cassowaries should never be approached or fed by people. Read more about [dangerous animals](/topics/dangerous-animals).

## Life cycle and chick rearing

A female cassowary lays **3 to 5 large green eggs** and then leaves. The **male** takes over entirely—incubating the eggs for about 50 days, then raising the chicks alone for up to 16 months.

Cassowary chicks are striped brown and cream for camouflage. The male defends them aggressively and teaches them foraging skills. Females may mate with several males in a single season.

## How big and fast are cassowaries?

Females are larger than males, reaching up to **5.6 feet (1.7 m)** tall and weighing up to 167 pounds (76 kg). Cassowaries can sprint at up to **31 mph (50 km/h)** through dense forest and are strong swimmers.

Their feathers look more like coarse hair than typical bird feathers. Dense, double-shafted plumage protects them from thorny undergrowth and rain in the humid rainforest.

## Why are cassowaries at risk?

Cassowaries are listed as [Vulnerable](/conservation-status/vulnerable). **Deforestation** in Australia and New Guinea continues to shrink their habitat. In Queensland, vehicle strikes on roads that cut through forest are a leading cause of death.

Dogs and pigs prey on chicks and eggs. Cassowaries are sometimes illegally captured for trade. Conservation programs focus on habitat protection, road warning signs, and community education near cassowary zones.
