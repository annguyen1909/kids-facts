## What is a jaguar?

A jaguar is the **largest wild cat in the Americas** and the third largest cat in the world. It has a muscular body, a broad head with powerful jaws, and a coat covered in dark **rosette spots**. No two jaguars have exactly the same spot pattern.

Jaguars belong to the cat family [Felidae](/families/felidae). Their scientific name is *Panthera onca*, placing them in the same genus as lions, tigers, and leopards.

## Where do jaguars live?

Jaguars live across **South and Central America**, with the largest population in Brazil's Amazon Basin. Smaller groups survive in Mexico, Bolivia, Peru, Colombia, and parts of Central America.

They prefer dense **tropical rainforests** and **wetlands** like the Pantanal, where water and large prey are available year-round. Unlike most big cats, jaguars actively seek out rivers and lakes. Explore more [rainforest animals](/habitats/rainforest).

## What do jaguars eat?

Jaguars are [carnivores](/diets/carnivore) that hunt a wide variety of animals. Their prey includes capybara, deer, peccary, caiman, fish, and turtles.

Their most remarkable trait is their **jaw strength**—one of the most powerful of any cat relative to body size. A jaguar can bite directly through a turtle's shell or pierce the skull of its prey in a single strike rather than suffocating it like most big cats do.

## How do jaguars hunt?

Jaguars are **ambush hunters**. They stalk through dense cover, waiting for the right moment to burst forward in a short, explosive attack. Their spotted coat blends perfectly with dappled forest light.

They also hunt in **water**, wading or swimming into rivers to catch fish and caiman. Strong, slightly webbed paws help them move through wetland terrain that other large predators avoid.

## Life cycle and cubs

A baby jaguar is called a **cub**. A mother gives birth to one to four cubs in a hidden den among rocks or thick vegetation. Cubs are born blind and completely depend on their mother.

Cubs stay with their mother for up to **two years**, learning to stalk, swim, and make kills. Males play no role in raising cubs. Young jaguars must establish their own territory before they can begin hunting independently.

## How do jaguars compare to leopards?

Jaguars and leopards look similar, but jaguars are **stockier and heavier**. A jaguar's rosettes have small dark spots inside them, while a leopard's rosettes are usually plain. Jaguars also have a larger, rounder head and a shorter tail.

Most importantly, jaguars live in **the Americas** while leopards live in Africa and Asia. Jaguars can also roar—a deep, repetitive sound used to mark territory and communicate. Read more about [social animals](/topics/social-animals).

## Why are jaguars at risk?

Jaguars are listed as **Near Threatened**. Their range has shrunk by more than half in the past century because of deforestation, illegal hunting for their pelts, and conflict with cattle ranchers.

Conservation corridors connecting protected forests allow jaguars to move across large landscapes. Anti-poaching laws, community programs, and protected reserves in Brazil and Bolivia support the largest remaining wild populations.
