## What is a walrus?

A walrus is a giant **sea mammal** that lives in the cold Arctic. It has a round, blubbery body, flat flippers, a thick mustache of **whiskers**, and two long ivory **tusks**.

Walruses belong to the group of flippered sea mammals called pinnipeds, the same big group as seals and sea lions. The walrus's scientific name is *Odobenus rosmarus*, which means "tooth-walker."

## Where do walruses live?

Walruses live in the **Arctic**, in the cold seas around Alaska, Canada, Russia, Greenland, and Norway. They like **shallow water** where they can dive to the seafloor to find food.

To rest, walruses haul their heavy bodies out of the water onto **floating sea ice** or rocky beaches. When the ice melts in summer, huge numbers gather along the coasts.

## What do walruses eat?

Walruses are meat-eaters, but they do not hunt big animals. Instead, they dive to the dark seafloor to find **shellfish**, especially clams.

A walrus sweeps its **whiskers** across the mud to feel for buried clams. Then it uses powerful **suction**, slurping the soft animal right out of its shell. A single walrus can eat thousands of clams in one meal.

## How do walruses live together?

Walruses are very **social** animals. They gather in big, noisy groups called herds, sometimes piling up by the thousands on a beach or ice floe.

Huddling together keeps them warm and helps protect the calves in the middle of the group. Walruses grunt, bellow, and even make bell-like sounds to "talk" with one another.

## Life cycle and calves

A baby walrus is called a **calf**. A mother usually has just one calf, and she takes care of it for a long time, often two years or more.

Calves can swim soon after they are born and will ride on their mother's back when they get tired. They drink her rich milk and stay close until they are big enough to dive and feed on their own.

## Why are walruses at risk?

Walruses are listed as **Vulnerable**. Their biggest problem is **climate change**. As the Arctic warms, the sea ice they rest on is melting away.

Without ice near their feeding areas, walruses must crowd onto beaches, where calves can be hurt in stampedes. Protecting the Arctic and its ice helps keep walrus herds healthy.

## Tusks, whiskers, and blubber

A walrus's body is built for the cold. A thick layer of **blubber** keeps it warm in icy water, and its skin can look pale when cold and turn **pinkish** as it warms up in the sun.

Its two **tusks** are really giant teeth that keep growing through life. Walruses use them to pull themselves onto the ice, to show off to rivals, and to break breathing holes in the frozen sea.
