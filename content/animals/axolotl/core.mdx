## What is an axolotl?

An axolotl is a **salamander** native to Mexico that never grows up in the usual sense. Instead of transforming into a land-dwelling adult like most amphibians, it keeps its feathery external gills and aquatic lifestyle for its entire life. This trait is called **neoteny**.

Its scientific name is *Ambystoma mexicanum*. In Nahuatl—the language of the Aztecs—its name means roughly "water monster." Explore more [freshwater animals](/habitats/freshwater).

## Where do axolotls live?

Wild axolotls exist only in the **canal system of Lake Xochimilco** within Mexico City. This is one of the most restricted ranges of any vertebrate on Earth. The ancient lake once spread across the Valley of Mexico, but urban expansion has reduced it to a network of canals.

The water is cool, shallow, and weedy—ideal for an animal that hides among aquatic plants and hunts along the muddy bottom. [Critically endangered](/conservation-status/critically-endangered) in the wild, axolotls survive in enormous numbers in captivity worldwide.

## What do axolotls eat?

Axolotls are [carnivores](/diets/carnivore) that ambush prey rather than chase it. They eat **worms, insect larvae, small fish, crustaceans, and mollusks**—virtually anything small enough to fit in their wide mouths.

They feed using suction: they open their mouths rapidly to create a vacuum that draws prey in. A sensitive **lateral line system** detects water vibrations, helping them locate prey in murky water even without relying on sight.

## The axolotl's remarkable regeneration

The axolotl is one of the most regenerative animals known to science. A lost limb regrows completely—bone, muscle, blood vessels, and nerves—within weeks, with almost no scarring.

Even more remarkable, axolotls can regenerate **heart tissue, spinal cord segments, and parts of their brain**. Researchers around the world study them to understand how such tissue repair works, hoping it may one day inform treatments for human injuries. ## Life cycle and eggs

Axolotls reach sexual maturity while still in their larval form—a consequence of neoteny. A female lays **100 to 1,000 eggs** attached to aquatic plants. The eggs hatch in 10 to 14 days.

Hatchlings are tiny and vulnerable. They are cared for by neither parent and must immediately hunt microscopic prey. Axolotls grow steadily over their first year and can live 10 to 15 years.

## How big are axolotls?

Most axolotls reach **6 to 12 inches** (15–30 cm) in length, though some grow as long as 18 inches (45 cm). Their most striking features are the three pairs of **feathery external gills** that fan out from the sides of their head, used to extract oxygen from water.

Color varies widely. Wild axolotls are dark with golden speckles. Captive breeding has produced leucistic (pale pink), melanoid (black), and golden albino forms.

## Why are axolotls at risk?

Wild axolotls face severe threats. **Pollution and water drainage** from Mexico City have degraded Lake Xochimilco. Invasive species—especially tilapia and carp—compete for food and prey on eggs and juveniles.

Conservation efforts include canal clean-up projects, "axolotl sanctuaries" in protected canal sections, and breeding programs. Without continued intervention, the axolotl could vanish from its only wild home.
