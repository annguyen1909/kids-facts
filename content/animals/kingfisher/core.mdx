## What is a common kingfisher?

The common kingfisher is a small, brightly colored bird famous all over the world for its brilliant plumage and spectacular fishing skills. Known scientifically as *Alcedo atthis*, this sparrow-sized bird is also called the **Eurasian kingfisher** or the **river kingfisher**.

Despite their small size, kingfishers are easy to recognize because of their striking colors. They have a bright blue-green back and wings, a vivid copper-orange breast, and a very long, black, dagger-like bill. Surprisingly, their feathers aren't actually blue! The blue color is created by the way light bounces off the structure of their feathers, a trick called structural coloration.

As birds, common kingfishers are warm-blooded, have wings and feathers, lay eggs, and belong to the kingfisher family Alcedinidae.

## Where do common kingfishers live?

Common kingfishers have a massive geographic range, living across **Eurasia and North Africa**. They are found throughout Europe, across Russia and Central Asia, and down into India, China, Japan, and Southeast Asia.

These birds are strictly tied to freshwater environments. They prefer **slow-moving rivers, streams, lakes, ponds, and canals** that are clear and clean. They also need trees, reeds, or bushes overhanging the water's edge, which they use as perches to watch for fish. In winter, if freshwater sources freeze over, some kingfishers will migrate to warmer coastal estuaries and marshes.

## What do common kingfishers eat?

Common kingfishers are **carnivores** that specialize in catching aquatic creatures. Their favorite food is **small fish**, especially minnows, sticklebacks, and young trout.

However, they are not picky eaters. They also hunt water insects like dragonfly larvae and water beetles, as well as tadpoles, frogs, and small freshwater shrimp. They use their excellent vision to spot prey swimming just below the surface of the water, even adjusting for the way water bends light so they never miss.

## How do common kingfishers behave?

Common kingfishers are solitary and highly territorial birds. Each kingfisher claims a stretch of river—usually about 1 to 2 kilometers long—and will aggressively chase away any other kingfishers that try to hunt there.

Their most famous behavior is their **hunting dive**. A kingfisher sits patiently on a branch overhanging the water, watching for movement. Once it spots a fish, it dives headfirst into the water, closing a transparent third eyelid (called a nictitating membrane) over its eyes to act like swimming goggles. It grabs the fish with its beak, beats its wings to return to the surface, and flies back to its perch. 

Before eating, the kingfisher beats the fish against the branch to stun it. Then, it swallows the fish **whole and head-first**, so the fins and scales fold down smoothly and don't scratch its throat.

## Life cycle and baby common kingfishers

Unlike most birds, kingfishers do not build nests out of twigs in trees. Instead, they use their sharp bills and strong feet to dig **tunnels into steep sandy riverbanks**. These nesting tunnels can be up to 3 feet (1 meter) long and end in a safe, hollow chamber.

The female lays a clutch of **2 to 10 glossy white eggs**. Both parents take turns sitting on the eggs to keep them warm for about 19 to 21 days until they hatch. 

The baby chicks are born blind and featherless. They grow quickly, fed constantly by their parents with tiny fish. After about 24 to 27 days, the young kingfishers leave the tunnel. The parents teach them how to fish for a few days, but then quickly chase them out of the territory so they can find their own stretch of river.

## Why are common kingfishers at risk?

Globally, the common kingfisher is listed as a species of **Least Concern** because of its huge range and stable populations. However, they face significant local threats.

Because they rely on sight to hunt, they need **clean, clear water**. Water pollution from chemicals, sewage, or soil runoff makes rivers murky, making it impossible for kingfishers to find food. Habitat loss, such as clearing trees from riverbanks or building concrete canal walls, also destroys their nesting sites and perches.

Additionally, very cold winters can freeze the surfaces of rivers and lakes, preventing them from fishing and causing high mortality. Protecting our waterways helps ensure these colorful fishermen thrive.
