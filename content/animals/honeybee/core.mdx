## What is a honey bee?

A honey bee is a small flying insect belonging to the family Apidae. They are famous for their **striped yellow and black bodies**, fuzzy hairs, and ability to build wax nests called hives. As insects, they have six legs, three main body parts (head, thorax, and abdomen), and two pairs of wings.

Their scientific name is *Apis mellifera*. Honey bees are extremely social creatures and are among the most studied insects on Earth because of their complex cooperative behavior.

## Where do honey bees live?

Honey bees are originally native to Europe, Africa, and parts of Asia, but humans have brought them to **every continent except Antarctica**. They can live in a wide range of habitats, including **forests, grasslands, meadows, deserts, and farms**.

In the wild, they construct nests inside hollow trees, rock crevices, or hanging under cliffs. In managed environments, they live in wooden hives cared for by beekeepers.

## What do honey bees eat?

Honey bees are **herbivores** that get all their food from flowering plants. Their diet consists of two main items:
- **Nectar**: A sweet liquid produced by flowers. Bees drink nectar for energy and carry it back to the hive to turn into honey.
- **Pollen**: A powdery dust found in flowers. Workers carry pollen back in special "baskets" on their hind legs. Pollen is full of protein and is fed to growing bee larvae.

## How do honey bees live together?

Honey bees live in large family groups called **colonies**, with up to 60,000 bees in a single hive. Every bee has a specific job:
- **The Queen**: The mother of all the bees. She lays up to 2,000 eggs every day.
- **Worker Bees**: Female bees that do all the work, including foraging, nursing babies, building honeycomb, and guarding the hive.
- **Drones**: Male bees whose only job is to fertilize new queens.

Worker bees also communicate the location of flowers by doing a figure-eight **"waggle dance"** on the comb!

## Life cycle and baby bees

The life of a honey bee begins as a tiny egg laid by the queen in a wax cell. After three days, the egg hatches into a **larva**, which looks like a tiny white worm. 

Worker bees feed the larva royal jelly and bee bread. Eventually, they seal the cell with wax, and the larva spins a cocoon to become a **pupa**. Inside the cocoon, it transforms, eventually chewing its way out as a fully grown adult bee.

## Why are honey bees at risk?

Honey bees face several threats in the wild, including **pesticides, disease, and habitat loss** as natural fields and wildflowers are replaced by cities and large farms. 

This decline is worrying because bees are **critical pollinators**. They pollinate one-third of the crops we eat, such as apples, berries, and almonds. To help, many people plant native wildflowers and avoid using chemical pesticides in their gardens.
