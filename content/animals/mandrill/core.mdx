## What is a mandrill?

The mandrill is the world's largest monkey—a powerfully built primate famous for the extraordinary **blue and red facial coloring** of adult males. It lives in the dense [tropical rainforests](/habitats/tropical-rainforest) of Central Africa.

Its scientific name is *Mandrillus sphinx*. Despite its baboon-like appearance, the mandrill belongs to its own genus and is more closely related to mangabeys than to true baboons.

## Where do mandrills live?

Mandrills live in tropical lowland rainforests in Cameroon, Gabon, the Republic of Congo, and Equatorial Guinea. They are most common in undisturbed primary forest with dense undergrowth.

They spend most of their time **on the forest floor**, foraging through leaf litter and digging for roots and insects. At night they climb into trees to sleep safely away from ground predators. Explore more [African rainforest animals](/topics/african-animals).

## What do mandrills eat?

Mandrills are [omnivores](/diets/omnivore) with a diet dominated by fruit, seeds, roots, and fungi. They also eat insects, snails, eggs, and occasionally small vertebrates such as lizards and frogs.

One remarkable adaptation is their large **cheek pouches**, which can hold nearly as much food as the stomach itself. Mandrills stuff food into their cheeks while foraging and consume it later in a safer location.

## Social life and hordes

Mandrills live in some of the largest social groups of any monkey, known as a **horde**. A horde can number from a few dozen up to over 800 individuals—a massive assembly moving through the forest together.

Within the horde, adult males compete intensely for dominance. A male's **facial coloring becomes more vivid** as he rises in rank, advertising his status visually. High-ranking males gain priority access to food and mating opportunities. Read more about [social animals](/topics/social-animals).

## Coloring and communication

The adult male mandrill has the most striking coloration of any mammal. Blue bony ridges flank his bright **red nose**, while his rump displays matching blue and red patches. These colors signal rank and emotional state—they literally brighten with excitement.

Females and young are much more subdued in coloring. Males groom each other and display their canine teeth in elaborate rituals to avoid physical conflict within the horde.

## Life cycle and young

Females give birth to a single **infant** after a pregnancy of about 175 days. Infants cling to their mother's belly immediately after birth and remain close to her for over a year.

Young mandrills grow slowly and learn foraging skills and social behaviors by watching adults. Males become sexually mature at about 9 years and can live **20 to 25 years** in the wild, with captive individuals reaching 40 years.

## Conservation

Mandrills are listed as [Vulnerable](/conservation-status/vulnerable). The main threats are **deforestation** from logging and agricultural expansion across their Central African range, and heavy hunting for the bushmeat trade.

Mandrills reproduce slowly and require large tracts of intact rainforest to sustain healthy hordes. National parks in Gabon and Cameroon protect key populations, and international bushmeat trade regulations are critical to their long-term survival.
