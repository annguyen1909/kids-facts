## What is a blue-footed booby?

The blue-footed booby (*Sula nebouxii*) is an iconic marine bird that captures the imagination of wildlife enthusiasts worldwide. Belonging to the Sulidae family, which includes gannets and other booby species, this seabird is renowned for its unmistakable bright turquoise-blue webbed feet, sharp serrated beak, and comical waddling gait on land.

The word "booby" originates from the old Spanish word *bobo*, meaning "silly" or "foolish." Early European sailors coined the term because the birds appeared exceptionally clumsy on the ship decks and exhibited an extreme lack of fear toward humans. Despite their awkward terrestrial manners, blue-footed boobies are highly dynamic and graceful masters of the air and sea.

## Where does it live?

Blue-footed boobies inhabit the tropical and subtropical coastal regions of the eastern Pacific Ocean. While their range extends from the Pacific coast of Mexico down through Peru, the undisputed stronghold of the species is the Galapagos Islands in Ecuador. Over half of the global breeding population establishes colonies across these legendary volcanic islands.

They spend the majority of their lives soaring over open coastal waters in search of food, returning to land strictly to roost and breed. For nesting grounds, they favor arid, rocky shorelines, coastal cliffs, and sparse scrublands where ground nests can be established away from terrestrial predators.

## What does it eat?

As specialized marine carnivores, blue-footed boobies rely entirely on ocean fisheries for their sustenance. Their preferred diet consists of small, densely schooling pelagic fish, with Pacific sardines and anchovies acting as their most vital staples. They also consume mackerel, flying fish, and squid when available.

Their hunting technique is a spectacular display of avian athleticism: plunge-diving. Boobies fly at elevations of 30 to 100 feet (10 to 30 meters) above the sea surface, scanning the water with sharp binocular vision. Once a fish school is spotted, the bird pins its wings against its body and plummets like a streamlined dart into the water at speeds reaching 60 miles per hour, snatching the stunned fish underwater and swallowing it before breaking the surface.

## How does it behave?

The social behavior of the blue-footed booby is defined by its world-famous courtship rituals. To secure a mate, the male executes an elaborate high-stepping dance. He struts proudly in front of the female, lifting his vibrant blue webbed feet high into the air one after the other. He then angles his head and tail directly toward the sky in a posture known as "sky-pointing" while emitting soft, piping whistles.

Outside of courtship, boobies are highly cooperative foragers. When a single bird spots a massive school of fish, it signals to the flock, resulting in spectacular synchronized plunging displays where dozens of boobies dive into the sea in unison.

## Life cycle and babies

Unlike many seabirds that construct elaborate arboreal nests, blue-footed boobies establish simple shallow depressions directly on the rocky ground, surrounded by rings of accumulated guano. The female lays between one and three chalky white eggs, which both parents diligently incubate for 40 to 45 days using the vascularized webbing of their warm feet rather than a brood patch.

Newly hatched chicks are entirely altricial, hatching blind and naked before quickly growing a dense coat of fluffy white down. Both parents work tirelessly to regurgitate digested fish for the growing brood. If food supplies become scarce during poor ocean upwelling events, older dominant chicks may outcompete or eliminate their younger siblings. The surviving juveniles fledge after roughly four months and achieve full adult plumage and brilliant blue feet within two to three years.

## Why is it at risk?

The blue-footed booby is officially classified as Least Concern on the IUCN Red List, maintaining a relatively stable overall population across its global range. However, local populations within the Galapagos Islands face persistent environmental and anthropogenic pressures.

The single greatest natural threat to their breeding success is the El Niño-Southern Oscillation. During severe El Niño years, warm ocean water displaces nutrient-rich cool currents, causing sardine populations to collapse and leading to widespread breeding failure among booby colonies. Furthermore, commercial overfishing of anchovies and sardines across the eastern Pacific directly depletes their essential food supply. Protecting coastal marine fisheries is crucial for ensuring these colorful symbols of the Galapagos continue to thrive.
