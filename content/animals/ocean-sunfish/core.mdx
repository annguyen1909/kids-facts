## What is an ocean sunfish?

The ocean sunfish (*Mola mola*) is a biological marvel and the heaviest known bony fish on the planet. Recognized instantly by its unusual, truncated appearance, this oceanic giant looks as though it was cut in half right behind its large pectoral fins. Instead of a traditional tapering tail, it features a rounded, rudder-like structure called a clavus, giving it the appearance of a massive swimming head.

Belonging to the order Tetraodontiformes, sunfish share close evolutionary ties with pufferfish and porcupinefish. Despite their alien silhouette and awkward appearance on land, they are remarkably graceful underwater, propelling themselves by flapping their elongated dorsal and anal fins like a pair of synchronous wings.

## Where does it live?

Ocean sunfish inhabit the pelagic zone of tropical and temperate oceans across the globe. They are highly migratory wanderers, crossing extensive oceanic expanses in the Atlantic, Pacific, and Indian Oceans to follow warm water currents and seasonal food concentrations.

These fish spend the majority of their lives in the open ocean, far from coastal reef structures. They navigate across a vast vertical water column, spending time cruising near the sunlit surface layer before plunging to depths exceeding 2,000 feet (600 meters) to forage in the cold, dark bathypelagic zone.

## What does it eat?

Sunfish are prolific open-ocean foragers whose diet centers heavily around gelatinous zooplankton, particularly jellyfish, salps, and comb jellies. Because jellyfish are composed of over 90% water and contain very few calories, a giant sunfish must consume immense quantities daily to maintain its colossal body bulk.

In addition to gelatinous prey, sunfish actively suck in squid, small schooling fish, eel larvae, and algae. Their mouths feature teeth fused into a strong, immovable parrot-like beak, preventing them from fully closing their jaws but allowing them to shred slippery prey with ease.

## How does it behave?

The most famous behavior of the Mola mola is surface basking. Sunfish frequently float completely flat on their sides at the ocean surface, drifting calmly in the bright sunshine. This behavior serves a crucial biological purpose: it acts as a solar recharging station, allowing the fish to warm its core body temperature back up after conducting deep foraging dives in frigid water.

Basking also provides an excellent opportunity for cleaning. Sunfish harbor dozens of external parasite species on their thick, leathery skin. By floating at the surface or visiting coastal kelp beds, sunfish allow cleaner wrasses, and even seabirds like albatrosses and gulls, to feast on the troublesome parasites attached to their bodies.

## Life cycle and babies

The reproductive capacity of the ocean sunfish is unmatched in the vertebrate world. During broadcast spawning events in open waters, a single mature female can release up to 300 million tiny eggs into the water column at one time, where they are externally fertilized by males.

Upon hatching, sunfish larvae look nothing like their giant adult counterparts. Measuring just 2.5 millimeters in length, the miniature fry possess large pectoral fins, a distinct tail fin, and a body covered in sharp star-like spines for protection. As they mature, the spines disappear, the true tail fin vanishes, and the fish undergoes a monumental growth spurt, multiplying its original body mass by over 60 million times!

## Why is it at risk?

The ocean sunfish is classified as Vulnerable on the IUCN Red List, with global populations experiencing notable declines. The primary threat to their survival is commercial fishing operations. Although sunfish are rarely targeted intentionally, they represent a massive percentage of accidental bycatch in offshore drift gillnets and longline fisheries targeting swordfish and tuna.

Furthermore, ocean pollution poses a severe, lethal hazard. Floating plastic bags look exactly like drifting jellyfish in the water column; when ingested by a sunfish, the plastic causes fatal digestive blockages or starvation. Increasing international regulation of offshore driftnets and global efforts to curb plastic waste are crucial for protecting these peaceful giants of the open ocean.
