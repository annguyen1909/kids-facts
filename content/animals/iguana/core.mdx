## What is a green iguana?

The green iguana is a large, lizard-like reptile that belongs to the iguanid family. Known scientifically as *Iguana iguana*, it is one of the most common and recognizable lizard species in the world. 

Although they are called "green" iguanas, these reptiles can come in many different colors! Depending on where they live, their age, and the temperature, green iguanas can be bright green, bluish-gray, orange, or even reddish-brown. They are famous for the long row of sharp-looking (but actually flexible) spines that run along their back and tail, which help them look larger to predators. They also have a large flap of skin under their chin called a **dewlap**, which they use to control their body temperature and talk to other iguanas.

As reptiles, green iguanas are cold-blooded, have dry scaly skin, and lay eggs on land.

## Where do green iguanas live?

Green iguanas are native to the warm, tropical regions of **Central and South America**, as well as parts of the Caribbean islands. 

They are highly **arboreal** animals, which means they spend most of their lives high up in the forest canopy. They live in wet tropical rainforests, gallery forests, and open savannas. They almost always live near water, such as rivers, lakes, or streams. They are excellent swimmers and will dive from high branches directly into the water below to escape predators or move to new feeding spots.

In some places, like Florida, Puerto Rico, and Hawaii, green iguanas have been introduced by humans and have become an invasive species because they thrive so well in warm climates.

## What do green iguanas eat?

Green iguanas are almost strictly **herbivores**, which means they eat only plants. Unlike many other lizards that eat insects or small animals, green iguanas eat leaves, flowers, fruits, and young plant shoots.

They love eating leaves from tropical trees, especially wild plums, hibiscus flowers, and various jungle fruits. When they are very young, they might occasionally eat small insects or snails, but as they grow, they shift entirely to a plant-based diet. 

Because they eat leaves, they have sharp, leaf-shaped teeth that act like tiny saws to shred tough plant material. They get almost all the water they need from the plants they eat and the morning dew on leaves.

## How do green iguanas behave?

Green iguanas are active during the day (diurnal) and spend a lot of their time **basking in the sun**. Because they are cold-blooded, they need the sun's warmth to raise their body temperature and digest their food. They will sit on high tree branches overhanging water for hours to soak up the sunlight.

To communicate, green iguanas use visual signals. They bob their heads up and down to show dominance or defend their territory. They also puff up their dewlaps to make themselves look bigger and more impressive. 

If they are threatened, they are excellent at defending themselves. They can whip their long, muscular tails with great force, use their sharp claws, or deliver a painful bite. But their first choice is usually to run away or leap into the water to swim away.

## Life cycle and baby green iguanas

Female green iguanas dig nests in sandy soil or riverbanks during the dry season. A female can lay a very large clutch of **20 to 70 eggs** in a single nest! After laying the eggs, she covers the nest and leaves, providing no care for the babies.

The eggs incubate in the warm ground for about 90 to 120 days. When the baby iguanas (called **hatchlings**) emerge, they are bright green and measure about 7 inches (18 cm) long. 

Unlike many other reptiles, baby iguanas often stay together in small groups for the first few months of their lives. This group behavior helps protect them from predators. They grow quickly, but it takes several years for them to reach their full adult size.

## Why are green iguanas at risk?

Green iguanas are listed as a species of **Least Concern** globally because they are very common and have large populations. However, they still face threats in their native habitats.

The biggest threat to wild green iguanas is **habitat destruction**, as forests are cleared for farming, cattle ranching, and building roads. In some parts of Central and South America, they are also hunted by humans for their meat and eggs (often called "bamboo chicken"), and captured for the illegal pet trade.

Fortunately, they are very hardy animals that reproduce quickly, which helps their populations remain stable in the wild.
