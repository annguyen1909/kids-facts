## What is a toucan?

A toucan is a beautiful, exotic bird belonging to the family Ramphastidae. They are famous all over the world for their **massive, colorful bills**, which can look almost too big for their bodies!

Despite its giant size, a toucan's bill is surprisingly light. It is hollow on the inside, supported by a network of thin bone fibers, and made of keratin—the same material as human fingernails. Their feathers are mostly shiny black with a bright white throat and chest, making their yellow, orange, and red beak stand out even more.

Their scientific name is *Ramphastos toco*, which refers to the Toco Toucan, the largest and most famous of all toucan species. As birds, they are warm-blooded, lay eggs, and have feathers and wings.

## Where do toucans live?

Toucans are native to the tropical regions of **South America**. They can be found in countries like Brazil, Argentina, Bolivia, Paraguay, and Guyana.

Unlike many other rainforest birds, toucans are not found deep inside dense, dark forests. Instead, they prefer **open forest canopies, woodlands, savannas, and plantations** where there are plenty of fruiting trees. 

Toucans do not build nests by digging holes. Instead, they live in **natural tree hollows** or holes created by woodpeckers. Several toucans will squeeze together inside one small hollow to sleep, folding their tails over their backs to save space.

## What do toucans eat?

Toucans are **omnivores** with a strong preference for fruit. Their diet is mostly made up of tropical fruits, such as figs, guavas, bananas, and palm berries. 

Using the very tip of their long bills, they pluck fruit from branches. Then, they throw their head back and **toss the fruit into the air**, catching it in their throat to swallow it whole! 

Toucans also need protein to stay healthy. They will eat insects, frogs, small lizards, and occasionally raid other birds' nests to eat their eggs or small chicks. They get almost all their water from the fruit they eat, so they rarely need to drink.

## How do toucans behave?

Toucans are very active and noisy birds. They travel through the canopy in **small, loose flocks** of about six to twelve birds. They call out to each other with loud, frog-like croaks or rattling sounds that can be heard throughout the forest.

Because their wings are relatively short, toucans are not very good at flying long distances. Instead of soaring, they fly in short, heavy bursts and prefer to **hop from branch to branch** using their strong legs.

They are also very playful. Friendly toucans will engage in **bill wrestling**, where they gently clash their beaks together, or play catch by tossing fruit to one another.

## Life cycle and baby toucans

Toucans form pairs during the breeding season. The female lays **2 to 4 white eggs** inside a tree hollow. Both parents take turns sitting on the eggs to keep them warm for about 16 to 18 days until they hatch.

Baby toucans, called **chicks**, are born completely naked, blind, and helpless. They do not have the iconic large bill of their parents; their bills are small and grow slowly as they mature.

Both the mother and father feed the chicks fruit and insects. The chicks grow quickly and start to grow feathers. After about **six to eight weeks**, the young toucans are strong enough to leave the tree hollow and learn to hop and fly on their own.

## Why are toucans at risk?

Toco toucans are currently listed as Least Concern, which means they are not currently in danger of extinction. However, their wild populations are decreasing.

The biggest threat they face is **habitat loss**. Logging, agriculture, and forest fires are destroying the large, old trees they need for shelter and fruit. Without tree hollows, toucans cannot lay their eggs safely.

They are also captured from the wild for the **illegal pet trade** because of their beautiful appearance. Protecting South America's woodlands and preventing illegal trapping are critical steps in ensuring toucans remain a colorful part of our planet.
