## What is a blue whale?

A blue whale is a **marine mammal** and the largest animal on Earth. Its long, streamlined body is blue-gray, and it has a broad, flat head with two blowholes on top.

Blue whales belong to the rorqual family of baleen whales. Their scientific name is *Balaenoptera musculus*. Instead of teeth, they use **baleen plates** to filter food from seawater.

## Where does it live?

Blue whales roam **open oceans** around the world. They migrate long distances between cold polar feeding grounds and warmer waters where they breed and raise calves.

You can find them in the North Atlantic, North Pacific, Southern Ocean, and Indian Ocean. Countries such as the United States, Chile, Sri Lanka, and Australia are among the places where people sometimes spot them from boats.

## What does it eat?

Blue whales are [carnivores](/diets/carnivore) that eat animals—but almost all of their food is tiny. They feed mainly on **krill**, small shrimp-like creatures that swarm in cold, nutrient-rich seas.

A blue whale opens its huge mouth, gulps water packed with krill, and pushes the water out through its baleen. The bristly baleen traps the krill inside, and the whale swallows them.

## How does it behave?

Most of the time, blue whales swim **alone** or in small groups. Mothers and calves stay close together for many months while the calf grows stronger.

Blue whales communicate with very low calls that can travel far underwater. During feeding season, several whales may gather where krill are especially abundant. Learn more about [social animals](/topics/social-animals).

## Life cycle and babies

A baby blue whale is called a **calf**. The mother gives birth in warmer ocean water after a pregnancy of about 10 to 12 months.

The calf nurses on rich milk and grows quickly—sometimes gaining about 200 pounds per day. It stays near its mother while learning to dive, surface, and find food on its own.

## How big and fast is it?

Blue whales are true giants. Many adults reach about **80 to 100 feet** long and can weigh up to 200 tons—heavier than a whole herd of elephants.

They usually swim slowly, but they can move fast when needed. In short bursts, a blue whale may swim up to about **30 mph** while traveling or escaping danger.

## Why is it at risk?

Blue whales are **Endangered**. Huge numbers were killed during commercial whaling in the 1900s, and some populations are still recovering.

Today, threats include ship strikes, ocean noise, fishing gear, and changes in krill supplies caused by climate shifts. Marine protected areas and safer shipping routes give blue whales a better chance to survive.
