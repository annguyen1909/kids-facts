## Meet the lion cub

A **cub** is a young lion from birth until roughly two years of age. Cubs are among the most photographed animals on Earth—and one of the most vulnerable stages in the [lion life cycle](/animals/lion/life-cycle).

Newborn cubs weigh only a few pounds. They cannot see, can barely walk, and depend entirely on their mother and pride for warmth, milk, and protection.

## Birth and the hidden den

Before cubs arrive, a pregnant lioness may leave the main pride group to find a **den**—a sheltered spot in thick bushes, tall grass, or rocks where predators cannot easily see or reach her litter.

After about **110 days** of pregnancy, she gives birth—usually to **two to four cubs**. The mother keeps cubs hidden for the first four to six weeks, visiting to nurse and moving them if danger appears.

### What newborns are like

- Blind for the first week or so
- Covered in soft fur, often with spots
- Blue-gray eyes that change color later
- Completely dependent on mother's milk

## Nursing and first meals

Mother's milk provides all nutrition for the first weeks. Cubs nurse frequently and grow quickly. By about three months, they begin tasting meat from kills brought near the den.

Weaning happens gradually. Cubs may nurse occasionally for many months even after eating solid food.

## Pride help: alloparenting

Lions are [social animals](/topics/social-animals). Related females in a pride often practice **alloparenting**—shared care of cubs.

Older females may:

- Guard cubs while mothers hunt
- Tolerate nursing from cubs that are not their own
- Carry cubs to new hiding spots

Shared care improves survival when food is scarce or predators are nearby.

## Play that prepares for adulthood

Cub life includes long hours of **play**. Siblings chase, pounce, wrestle, and ambush one another. Play looks fun, but it builds:

- Muscle strength
- Eye-paw coordination
- Hunting timing
- Social bonds within the pride

Cubs also learn by watching adults stalk, kill, and feed. Observation is a quiet classroom on the savanna.

Explore pride routines on the [lion behavior page](/animals/lion/behavior).

## Growing up: spots, size, and confidence

Cub spots often fade by adulthood, though faint markings may remain. Males begin growing manes as they approach two to three years. Females stay leaner and begin joining hunts as subadults.

By one year, cubs are much larger but still need protection. By two years, they are strong juveniles—old enough to follow hunts but not yet fully independent.

## Dangers cubs face

Cub mortality is high in many wild populations. Less than half of cubs may survive to adulthood in some areas. Threats include:

- **Hyenas and leopards** attacking unattended cubs
- **Pride takeovers** when new males kill existing young
- **Starvation** during drought or prey shortages
- **Disease** spreading in dense pride groups

These risks explain why pride living and shared care matter so much.

## Frequently asked questions

**Can you pet a wild lion cub?**  
No. Wild lions are dangerous predators, and approaching cubs puts you and the animals at risk.

**Do lion fathers help raise cubs?**  
Males protect territory, which indirectly protects cubs. Direct care is mostly from mothers and related females.

**Why do male lions sometimes hurt cubs?**  
When new males take over a pride, they may kill cubs so females breed again with the new leaders.
