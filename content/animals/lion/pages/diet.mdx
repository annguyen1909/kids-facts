## Are lions carnivores?

Yes. Lions are [carnivores](/diets/carnivore)—animals that eat other animals. Every part of a lion's body reflects that lifestyle: sharp canine teeth, slicing cheek teeth, strong neck muscles, and a digestive system designed to process meat, not plants.

In ecology, lions are often called **apex predators**. That means they hunt animals below them in the food web and are rarely hunted by other species as adults. Their feeding habits shape savanna ecosystems by influencing prey numbers and the behavior of other scavengers such as hyenas and vultures.

## What animals do lions eat?

Lions are **generalist hunters**. They target many species, but most prey are large hoofed mammals called **ungulates**. Common prey includes:

- **Zebra** — striped, fast, and often found in herds
- **Wildebeest** — migratory grazers that travel in huge groups
- **Antelope** — smaller and very quick, including impala and gazelle
- **African buffalo** — large and dangerous; hunting them is risky
- **Warthog** — smaller prey that lions can catch near burrows

Prey choice depends on what lives in the pride's territory, the season, and how many hunters are available. A pride with several strong lionesses can attempt larger prey. A smaller pride may focus on medium-sized animals.

Lions also **scavenge**. If they find an animal that died from injury, drought, or another predator's kill, they will eat it. Hyenas and lions often compete for carcasses on the savanna.

## How do lions hunt?

Lion hunting is a mix of patience, cover, and explosive speed. Lions are not built for long chases like cheetahs. They succeed when they get close before the chase begins.

A typical hunt follows several steps:

1. **Search** — Lions scan grasslands at dawn, dusk, or night when prey is active and temperatures are cooler.
2. **Stalk** — Hunters crouch low and use grass, bushes, and terrain to hide their approach.
3. **Ambush** — The group may spread out to block escape routes while one or more lions charge.
4. **Capture** — A lion grabs prey with claws and jaws, often aiming for the throat or muzzle to bring the animal down.
5. **Feed** — The pride eats quickly before hyenas or other scavengers arrive.

Lionesses do most of the hunting. Their lighter, leaner bodies are well suited to stalking and sprinting. Males join hunts sometimes, especially for very large prey, but they also guard territory and cubs.

Teamwork is critical. One lion alone struggles to kill a healthy adult zebra or wildebeest. Three or four coordinated hunters improve the odds dramatically.

## Why is teamwork so important?

Group hunting lets lions target prey that would be too big or too dangerous for a single cat. Buffalo, for example, can gore a lion with sharp horns. Multiple hunters can distract and attack from different angles.

Cooperation also saves energy. A failed hunt costs calories. Pride members that hunt together share knowledge of the land, prey paths, and seasonal movements—skills passed from older lions to younger ones through observation.

## Lion teeth and jaws

A lion's mouth is a powerful tool. The long **canines** stab and grip prey. The **carnassial teeth** shear meat like scissors. Lions do not chew food the way humans do; they slice off chunks and swallow them.

Bite force matters when subduing large animals. Strong jaw muscles help lions hold struggling prey until it stops moving. Their rough tongue—covered in tiny hooks—scrapes meat from bones so little food is wasted.

## When and how often do lions eat?

Lions do not follow a human meal schedule. After a large kill, a pride may feed for two or three days, resting near the carcass. Adults eat first when males are present, then females, then cubs.

Between meals, lions rest. Digestion takes time, and hunting is risky. An injury during a hunt can mean starvation for a lone hunter. In a pride, injured hunters may still receive food from group kills.

During dry seasons or when prey is scarce, lions hunt more often and may travel farther. When prey is abundant, they hunt less frequently.

## How does diet connect to habitat?

Lions eat what their habitat provides. [Savannas and grasslands](/habitats/savanna) support huge herds of zebra and wildebeest. Without open land and prey, lions cannot survive.

Water sources matter too. Prey gather at rivers and water holes, and lions patrol those areas. Habitat loss reduces prey numbers—and that is one reason lion populations are under pressure today.

## Lions and the food web

When lions hunt, they affect more than their prey. Scavengers feed on leftovers. Herd animals change where they graze when lions are nearby. This chain of interactions keeps ecosystems balanced.

Too few lions can mean overgrazing by herbivores. Too few prey can mean hungry prides and conflict with people. Healthy savannas need both predators and prey.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do lions eat fish or birds?**  
Rarely. Almost all of a lion's diet is mammal meat from hoofed animals.

**Do male lions hunt?**  
Sometimes, but lionesses do most hunting. Males often defend territory and cubs.

**Can lions survive without hunting?**  
Wild lions must hunt or scavenge. In human care, they are fed prepared meat.
