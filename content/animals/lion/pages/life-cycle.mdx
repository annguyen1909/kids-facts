## Overview of the lion life cycle

Every lion begins life as a small, helpless cub and—if it survives—grows into one of the savanna's most powerful predators. The lion life cycle has three main stages:

1. **Cub** — birth to about two years
2. **Juvenile / subadult** — roughly two to four years
3. **Adult** — from about four years onward, with peak strength in middle age

Lions are mammals. Females give birth to live young and nurse them with milk. Pride living shapes every stage: cubs are raised not just by their mother but often by the whole social group.

For close-up detail on newborns and nursing, see the [lion babies page](/animals/lion/babies).

## Birth and the cub stage

### Pregnancy and litter size

A lioness is pregnant for about **110 days**—a little over three months. She usually gives birth to a litter of **two to four cubs**, though litter size varies.

Before birth, the mother may leave the pride briefly to find a hidden den in thick bushes, tall grass, or a rocky crevice. Hidden dens protect blind, vulnerable newborns from hyenas, leopards, and rival lions.

### Newborn cubs

Cubs are born tiny—often weighing only 2–4 pounds (1–2 kg). They are blind at first and open their eyes after about a week. Their fur is spotted, which may help camouflage them in dappled shade.

For the first weeks, cubs stay at the den. The mother returns to nurse them and keeps them clean. Other females in the pride may help guard or transport cubs if the mother hunts.

### Nursing and early growth

Cubs nurse frequently. Mother's milk provides all the nutrition they need until they begin eating meat at around three months. By six months, meat makes up much of their diet, though nursing may continue longer.

Spots on cub fur often fade by adulthood, though some lions keep faint markings on their legs or belly.

## Play and learning

Cub life is not all rest. **Play** is how young lions build skills for adulthood. Siblings wrestle, chase, stalk grass, and pounce on one another's tails.

Through play, cubs develop:

- **Coordination** for hunting leaps
- **Strength** for holding prey
- **Social bonds** with pride members
- **Confidence** to follow adults on short walks

They also learn by watching. A cub that sees lionesses hunt begins to understand stalking, timing, and group positions long before it joins a real hunt.

Explore pride life on the [lion behavior page](/animals/lion/behavior).

## Juvenile stage: growing independence

Between roughly two and four years of age, young lions change roles. They are larger and stronger but not yet fully mature.

**Young females** often stay with their birth pride. They learn hunting skills from older lionesses and may help care for younger cubs—a behavior that strengthens pride bonds.

**Young males** face a different path. As they approach maturity, their manes begin to grow and they may be pushed out by dominant males. These nomadic young males—sometimes traveling in small groups called **coalitions**—must survive alone or with brothers until they can challenge for a pride of their own.

This stage is dangerous. Without pride protection, young males risk starvation, injury, and attacks from territorial adults.

## Adult stage

Adult lions are fully grown hunters and defenders. Females reach maturity around three to four years; males may take a year or two longer to reach full size and mane development.

### Adult females

Lionesses are the pride's core hunters and cub raisers. An adult female may give birth every two years when conditions are good, though cub survival varies.

### Adult males

Adult males defend territory, protect cubs from rival males, and join hunts for very large prey. A full mane signals maturity. Males in their prime are heavy, muscular, and intimidating to rivals.

### Peak years and aging

Lions are strongest in middle age—roughly five to ten years for many wild individuals. Older lions may lose territory to younger challengers. Injuries from hunts and fights accumulate. Teeth wear down, making feeding harder.

## How long do lions live?

**In the wild**, lions usually live **10 to 14 years**. Life is physically demanding. Hunger, injury, and conflict with other lions shorten many lives.

**In human care**—zoos and accredited sanctuaries—lions may live **18 to 20 years** or more. Regular food, veterinary care, and absence of territorial fights extend lifespan.

Males often have shorter wild lives than females because takeovers and fights are so dangerous.

## Survival through the life cycle

Not every cub reaches adulthood. Estimates suggest that in some populations, fewer than half of cubs survive their first year. Threats include:

- Hyenas and leopards attacking cubs
- Starvation when prey is scarce
- Disease
- Pride takeovers when new males kill existing cubs

This is why [social living](/topics/social-animals) matters. More adults watching, hunting, and defending territory means better odds for cubs.

Read about threats on the [predators and threats page](/animals/lion/predators-and-threats).

## Life cycle and diet

As lions grow, their diet shifts from milk to meat. Cubs first taste meat by eating from kills brought to the den. Older juveniles join the pride at carcasses. Young adults participate in hunts.

A lion's body changes with diet and exercise: stronger jaws, thicker bones, and—for males—a growing mane fueled by hormones and good nutrition.

## Frequently asked questions

**At what age can a lion hunt alone?**  
Lionesses usually join group hunts as subadults. Solo hunting is rare; pride cooperation is the norm.

**Do lion cubs have names?**  
Scientists identify cubs by pride, mother, and markings—not personal names.

**Can lions breed at any time of year?**  
Lions can breed year-round, though births may peak when prey is plentiful.
