## What is a manatee?

A manatee is a large, gentle [aquatic mammal](/topics/marine-mammals) that lives in warm shallow waters. Often called a **sea cow**, it spends its days slowly grazing on underwater plants and surfacing regularly to breathe.

Manatees belong to the order Sirenia and their scientific name is *Trichechus manatus*. Despite living in the ocean, their closest relatives on land are elephants and hyraxes.

## Where do manatees live?

Manatees live in warm, shallow [coastal waters](/habitats/coastal-ocean), rivers, estuaries, and lagoons in the Americas and West Africa. The West Indian manatee ranges from Florida and the Caribbean south to Brazil.

They cannot survive in cold water and must stay in areas above about 60°F (16°C). In winter, Florida manatees gather around natural warm springs and the heated discharge outlets of power plants. Explore more [ocean animals](/topics/ocean-animals).

## What do manatees eat?

Manatees are [herbivores](/diets/herbivore) that graze on **seagrass, water hyacinth, mangrove leaves, and algae**. An adult can eat 100 to 150 pounds (45 to 68 kg) of vegetation per day—about 10 percent of its body weight.

Their prehensile lips act like flexible fingers to grasp and pull plants from the seafloor. They graze slowly and methodically, moving through seagrass meadows much like cattle move through a pasture.

## Life cycle and calves

Female manatees give birth to a single **calf** after a pregnancy of about 12 to 14 months. Calves are born underwater and swim immediately to the surface to take their first breath.

Calves stay with their mothers for one to two years, learning migration routes and the locations of warm-water refuges. Manatees grow slowly and can live up to **60 years** in the wild. Read more about [slow-reproducing animals](/topics/conservation).

## Adaptations for aquatic life

Manatees have streamlined bodies, paddle-like forelimbs for steering, and a powerful flat tail for propulsion. Their low metabolic rate suits a diet of low-calorie plants.

One unusual adaptation: manatees continuously replace their **teeth**. Worn molars at the front fall out and are replaced by new molars moving forward from the back—an adaptation not seen in most other mammals.

## Breathing and behavior

Manatees must surface every **3 to 5 minutes** to breathe, although a resting individual can remain submerged for up to 20 minutes. They often sleep just below the surface, rising automatically to breathe without fully waking.

Manatees are generally solitary and peaceful. They communicate through squeaks, chirps, and squeals—especially between mothers and calves—and show curiosity toward humans in the water.

## Conservation

Manatees are listed as [Vulnerable](/conservation-status/vulnerable). The most common cause of death for Florida manatees is **boat propeller strikes**, and most adults carry permanent scars that scientists use to identify individuals by name.

Habitat loss from coastal development, harmful algal blooms that destroy seagrass beds, and entanglement in fishing gear are ongoing threats. Speed zones, wildlife sanctuaries, and seagrass restoration programs have helped populations recover slowly in recent decades.
