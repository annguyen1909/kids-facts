## What is a mute swan?

The mute swan is a very large, elegant waterbird famous all over the world for its graceful appearance, S-shaped neck, and pure white feathers. Known scientifically as *Cygnus olor*, it belongs to the same family as ducks and geese.

Mute swans are among the largest and simplest flying birds in the world. Adults have a wingspan of up to 8 feet (2.4 meters) and are completely covered in white feathers. They are easily recognized by their bright orange bills with a black knob at the base, and their elegant S-curved necks. Male swans (called cob) are slightly larger than females (called pen), but they look almost identical.

As waterbirds, mute swans are warm-blooded, covered in waterproof feathers, have webbed feet, and lay eggs.

## Where do mute swans live?

Mute swans are native to **Europe and Central Asia**. Because of their extreme beauty, they were introduced to North America, South Africa, Australia, and New Zealand by humans as decorative birds for parks and estates.

These birds live in shallow freshwater environments. They prefer **lakes, slow-moving rivers, marshes, ponds, and coastal lagoons**. They need bodies of water with plenty of underwater plants to eat and quiet, reedy shorelines where they can build their nests. In winter, if freshwater lakes freeze, they will migrate to ice-free coastal estuaries and sheltered bays.

## What do mute swans eat?

Mute swans are almost strictly **herbivores**, meaning they eat plants. Their diet is made up almost entirely of **aquatic plants**.

They love eating pondweed, wild celery, water grasses, and algae. They also forage on land in flooded fields, eating grass and grains like wheat or barley. Occasionally, they might accidentally swallow small water insects, tadpoles, or snails while feeding on plants, but these make up only a tiny part of their diet.

To feed, they do not dive underwater. Instead, they float on the surface, bend their long necks down, or tip their bodies forward into the water with their tails pointing straight up in the air (a behavior called up-ending) to reach plants growing on the muddy bottom.

## How do mute swans behave?

Mute swans are famous for their **graceful swimming** and peaceful appearance, but they are also known for their fierce and defensive nature. 

Although they are called "mute" because they are less vocal than other swan species, they are not completely silent. They communicate using soft snorting noises, whistling calls, and a very loud **hissing sound** when they are angry or threatened. 

During the breeding season, swans are highly territorial. A mated pair will aggressively defend their territory from other swans, ducks, and even humans. If an intruder gets too close to their nest, the male swan will puff up his wings, arch his neck back, hiss loudly, and swim rapidly toward the intruder. They can even flap their heavy, powerful wings to strike predators.

## Life cycle and baby mute swans

A swan pair usually **mates for life**. Together, they build a massive nest out of reeds, rushes, and grasses on the edge of the water or on a small island. These nests can be up to 6 feet (2 meters) wide!

The female lays a clutch of **5 to 8 large, pale green-grey eggs**. She sits on them for about 35 to 42 days to keep them warm until they hatch, while the male guards the nest.

Baby swans are called **cygnets**. When they hatch, they are covered in soft, fluffy grey or brown down and have black bills. Remarkably, cygnets can walk and swim just a few hours after hatching! To stay warm and safe from predators, cygnets will often climb up onto their parents' backs, riding cozy and dry between their wings. They stay with their parents for about 4 to 5 months until they learn to fly.

## Why are mute swans at risk?

The mute swan is listed as a species of **Least Concern** because their global population is large, healthy, and growing. In many areas, they are protected by laws that make it illegal to harm them.

However, they still face human-made threats. One of the biggest dangers to swans is **lead poisoning** from swallowing lead fishing weights or shotgun pellets left in the water, which they mistake for grit. They can also get tangled in discarded fishing lines or injure themselves by colliding with power lines while flying.

Water pollution from chemicals and oil spills can also damage their waterproof feathers, making it hard for them to swim and fly. Protecting our lakes and rivers helps keep these magnificent birds safe.
