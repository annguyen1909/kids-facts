## What is a grizzly bear?

A **grizzly bear** is a huge North American brown bear with a shoulder hump, long claws, and thick fur. Grizzlies are strong diggers and powerful runners despite their size.

Grizzly bears belong to the bear family Ursidae. Their scientific name is *Ursus arctos horribilis*.

## Where do grizzly bears live?

Grizzly bears live in **forests, river valleys, and mountains** across Alaska, western Canada, and parts of the northwestern United States. They need space to roam, food, and quiet den sites.

River valleys with salmon and berry patches are especially important feeding areas.

## What do grizzly bears eat?

Grizzly bears are **omnivores**. They eat berries, roots, grasses, insects, fish, and sometimes small mammals or carrion.

In salmon rivers, grizzlies may catch many fish during the spawning season to build fat for winter.

## How do grizzly bears live?

Adult grizzlies usually live **alone**, except when mothers raise cubs. In cold regions, they spend winter in dens and survive on stored fat.

Mother grizzlies are very protective and teach cubs how to find food and avoid danger.

## Why are grizzly bears at risk?

Grizzly bears are listed as **Least Concern** worldwide, but some southern populations are threatened by habitat loss and conflict with humans.

Protected parks and careful land planning help grizzlies keep room to roam and raise cubs.

## Life cycle and cubs

A baby grizzly bear is called a **cub**. Cubs are born tiny in winter dens while the mother is in deep sleep.

They stay with their mother for two to three years while learning to fish, forage, and survive on their own.
